BENO-X Framework Visualizer

The BENO-X constitutional analysis framework — click each step to understand the legal logic chain.

Exemption Exists

Does a valid exemption or authorization exist under ACMPR/MMAR at the time of the offence?

Is It Functional?

Was the exemption valid and operable — or was it functionally blocked by a regulatory defect?

►
3

Constitutional Consequence

If blocked: what Charter relief applies? Evidence exclusion, stay of proceedings, acquittal?

Step 1 — Exemption Exists: Under MMAR (repealed) or ACMPR, a person may hold a personal production licence or registered client licence. The BENO-X framework first establishes whether any valid authorization existed at the time of the alleged offence. Parker (2000) established that the right to possess cannabis for medical necessity is a constitutional right. Hitzig (2003) extended this. Document: licence number, issuance date, possession limits, production location authorization.
Step 2 — Functional Blockage Analysis: A licence may technically exist but be rendered non-functional by regulatory defects — Health Canada delays, administrative failures, or unconstitutional conditions that effectively denied access. The R. v. Owen (2010 ON) line of cases and Sfetkopoulos v. Canada (2008 FC) addressed regulatory non-compliance by the Crown. If the government's own failure created a legal gap, the prosecution cannot use that gap as its foundation.
Step 3 — Constitutional Consequence: Available Charter remedies include: (1) s.24(2) exclusion of evidence obtained in breach of ss.8/9; (2) stay of proceedings under s.24(1) where Crown misconduct is egregious; (3) acquittal if the Crown cannot prove the offence beyond reasonable doubt after exclusion; (4) constitutional challenge to the regulatory provision itself.
