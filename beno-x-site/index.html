The Core Methodology

The BENO-X Framework

A three-step analytical framework for evaluating regulatory exemption defects in constitutional defense contexts.

1
Exemption Exists

Does a regulatory exemption or authorization exist in law? What does the statute, regulation, or judicial remedy prescribe? Establish the legal landscape of the exemption.

→
2
Is It Functional?

Does the exemption actually work as a meaningful safeguard? Is it accessible, workable, and constitutionally compliant? A defective exemption may violate s.7 of the Charter.

→
3
Consequence

If the exemption is bad — defective, inaccessible, or unconstitutional — the resulting offense cannot stand. Bad Exemption = No Offense (BENO-X).

Foundational Jurisprudence

Case Law Foundation

Three pillars of Canadian jurisprudence that underpin the BENO-X analytical framework.

Ontario Court of Appeal · 2000
R v Parker

The Ontario Court of Appeal held that the blanket prohibition on cannabis possession violated s.7 of the Charter because it provided no exemption for medical users. The court struck down the prohibition and suspended the declaration to give Parliament time to create a workable medical exemption. The foundational BENO-X precedent: no valid exemption = no valid offense.

SCC · 2015 SCC 34
R v Smith

The Supreme Court of Canada unanimously held that restrictions on medical cannabis to dried plant form violated s.7 of the Charter. The exemption, as written, was not constitutionally adequate — patients required access to oils, edibles, and other forms. Smith extended the BENO-X principle: a narrow or defective exemption is as bad as no exemption.

Federal Court · 2016 FC 236
Allard v Canada

The Federal Court found that the MMPR regulatory framework violated patients' s.7 rights by eliminating personal production licences. Forcing patients to purchase from Licensed Producers at commercial prices — when they could otherwise grow their own — constituted an unconstitutional deprivation. Allard confirmed: workable exemptions must be accessible.
