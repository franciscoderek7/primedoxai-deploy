**Title:** Know Your s.10(b) Rights — Print This and Keep It in Your Wallet

**Body:**

If you've never memorized your Charter rights, now is the time. Section 10(b) of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms is the single most important right you have when you're arrested or detained.

Here's what it says and what you need to do:

---

**UPON ARREST OR DETENTION, YOU HAVE THE RIGHT TO:**

✅ Be informed promptly of the reason for your arrest or detention (s.10(a))

✅ Retain and instruct counsel without delay (s.10(b))

✅ Be informed of your right to a lawyer (s.10(b)) — police MUST tell you this

✅ Have access to legal aid information (Brydges duty) — even if you can't afford a lawyer

🗣 "I wish to speak to a lawyer. I am exercising my right to silence until I have done so."

---

**WHAT POLICE MUST DO:**

- Stop questioning you once you invoke your right to counsel
- Provide you with the opportunity to contact a lawyer
- In Canada, duty counsel is available 24/7 in most provinces (free)

If police continue to question you after you invoke s.10(b), any evidence obtained may be excluded at trial under s.24(2) of the Charter.

---

**WHY THIS MATTERS FOR CANNABIS USERS:**

Whether you're facing a simple possession charge, an impaired driving allegation, or a licensing-related investigation — invoking s.10(b) immediately and consistently is your most important legal protection.

Know your rights. Share this with someone who needs it.

## Facebook/Reddit Post 2: R v Smith (2015)

**Title:** R v Smith (2015): The Supreme Court Case That Expanded Medical Cannabis Access to Oils and Edibles

**Body:**

Before 2015, Canadian medical cannabis patients could legally possess dried cannabis — but extracts, oils, capsules, and edibles were prohibited, even with a medical authorization.

That changed with *R v Owen Smith* at the Supreme Court of Canada.

---

**BACKGROUND**

Owen Smith worked at a Victoria cannabis dispensary producing cookies, topicals, and oils for medical patients. He was charged with possession for the purpose of trafficking and production of cannabis resin.

His defence: the restriction to dried cannabis only violated patients' s.7 Charter rights (life, liberty, and security of the person).

---

**THE COURT'S FINDING**

A unanimous Supreme Court of Canada agreed.

Chief Justice McLachlin wrote that the restriction to dried cannabis forced patients to "consume cannabis in a manner that may be harmful to their health" — namely, by smoking rather than using safer, non-combustion alternatives.

The court held: the restriction to dried cannabis was arbitrary and grossly disproportionate to its objective, and therefore unconstitutional.

---

**WHAT CHANGED**

Health Canada had to expand the MMAR to allow medical patients to possess and use cannabis in forms other than dried flower — including oils, capsules, and other preparations.

This decision laid the direct legal groundwork for the full range of medical cannabis products available today.

---

*R v Smith* 2015 SCC 34 is required reading for anyone involved in Canadian cannabis law.

## Facebook/Reddit Post 3: ACMPR Patient Guide

**Title:** ACMPR Patient Guide: How to Get Authorized for Medical Cannabis in Canada (Step by Step)

**Body:**

More Canadians qualify for medical cannabis authorization than realize it. Here's the straightforward process:

---

**STEP 1: TALK TO YOUR HEALTHCARE PROVIDER**

Your family doctor or nurse practitioner can authorize medical cannabis. If they are reluctant or unfamiliar with cannabis medicine, you have options:
- Request a referral to a cannabis clinic
- Several telehealth platforms connect patients with cannabis-knowledgeable practitioners
- CCLDR.NET education resources can help you have an informed conversation

Conditions commonly authorized: chronic pain, PTSD, anxiety disorders, multiple sclerosis, cancer-related symptoms, epilepsy, sleep disorders, inflammatory conditions.

---

**STEP 2: OBTAIN YOUR MEDICAL DOCUMENT**

The medical document sets out:
- Your authorized daily amount in grams of dried cannabis
- Duration of the authorization
- Your healthcare provider's information

This is NOT a prescription in the traditional sense — it is an authorization.

---

**STEP 3: REGISTER WITH A LICENSED SELLER**

Take your medical document to a federally licensed medical cannabis seller (online mail-order is the most common format). They will register you and allow you to purchase your authorized quantities.

---

**STEP 4: OPTIONAL — APPLY FOR PERSONAL PRODUCTION**

If you want to grow your own:
- Apply to Health Canada for a personal production licence
- Plant count is calculated from your daily gram authorization
- Must grow at your primary or secondary residence
- Outdoor: fewer plants, more yield per plant; Indoor: more plants per gram authorized

---

**POSSESSION LIMITS (MEDICAL):**

Your medical possession limit = daily gram authorization × 30 days (or remaining days of authorization, whichever is less).

Example: Authorized for 3g/day = can legally possess up to 90g.

Compare to recreational limit of 30g in public.

---

Veterans: separate VAC coverage applies — see our Veterans post.

## Facebook/Reddit Post 4: Veterans and Cannabis — VAC Coverage

**Title:** Veterans and Cannabis: What Veterans Affairs Canada Actually Covers (and What It Doesn't)

**Body:**

Cannabis coverage through Veterans Affairs Canada (VAC) is real — but it's more limited than many veterans expect, and the policy has changed significantly since the early days of the program.

---

**WHAT VAC COVERS (as of 2026)**

✅ Up to **3 grams per day** of dried cannabis (or equivalent) — reduced from 10g/day previously
✅ Products from **federally licensed producers only**
✅ Dried flower, oils, capsules — authorized product formats
✅ Reimbursement processed through VAC exceptional care benefits

---

**WHAT VAC DOES NOT COVER**

❌ Products from unlicensed sources
❌ Amounts exceeding authorized daily limit
❌ Accessories (vaporizers, pipes, rolling papers)
❌ Cannabis for recreational purposes
❌ Automatic coverage — requires medical authorization and VAC approval

---

**HOW TO ACCESS COVERAGE**

1. Obtain a medical document from a physician or nurse practitioner authorizing cannabis for a diagnosed condition
2. Submit the medical document to VAC
3. VAC reviews and approves (or declines) coverage
4. If approved, register with a federally licensed seller and submit receipts for reimbursement

Most commonly approved conditions: PTSD, chronic pain, anxiety disorders, sleep disorders.

---

**IMPORTANT NOTE**

The 3g/day limit was significantly reduced from earlier policy. Some veterans who were previously authorized for higher daily amounts found their coverage reduced. Appeals are possible — consult a Veterans Affairs lawyer or advocate if your coverage was reduced and you believe your medical needs warrant more.

## Facebook/Reddit Post 5: Driving and Cannabis — The Numbers

**Title:** Driving and Cannabis: The Numbers You Need to Know Before You Get Behind the Wheel

**Body:**

Impaired driving is impaired driving — whether it's alcohol, cannabis, or anything else. Here are the specific numbers in Canadian law you need to know.

---

**THE LEGAL LIMITS (Blood THC)**

🔴 **5 ng/mL delta-9-THC** — indictable offence (most serious)
🟡 **2–5 ng/mL** — summary conviction offence
🔴 **Any detectable THC + alcohol** — combination offence

**ZERO TOLERANCE APPLIES TO:**
- Novice drivers (G1, G2, M1, M2)
- Commercial vehicle drivers
- School bus drivers
Any detectable THC = offence. No exceptions.

---

**HOW LONG DOES THC STAY IN YOUR BLOOD?**

This is where it gets complicated.

Blood THC levels drop rapidly after smoking — within 1–4 hours for most people. BUT impairment can persist beyond measurable blood THC levels, and heavy users may have elevated THC for days without recent use.

**Edibles:** THC enters blood more slowly but persists longer — do not drive for at least 4–6 hours after consuming edibles, and longer if you consumed a significant amount.

---

**ROADSIDE TESTING**

Standardized Field Sobriety Tests (SFST): police may administer if they have reasonable suspicion of impairment.

Approved Drug Screening Equipment (ADSE): oral fluid swab can detect recent THC use — a positive screen can lead to further blood testing.

Drug Recognition Experts (DREs): officers trained to evaluate impairment from drugs.

---

**THE SIMPLE RULE:**

If you've consumed cannabis, don't drive. Not worth the risk — to yourself, others, or your record.

## LinkedIn Article 1: Cannabis Compliance in Canada 2026

**Title:** The Business of Cannabis Compliance in Canada: 2026 Regulatory Update

**Body:**

For cannabis businesses in Canada, compliance is not a department — it is the foundation of the enterprise. The Cannabis Act and its regulations have created one of the most heavily regulated commercial sectors in Canadian history, and the compliance landscape continues to evolve in 2026.

**The Federal Framework**

Health Canada administers the Cannabis Act licensing regime, issuing approximately nine distinct licence classes from micro-cultivation to analytical testing. What distinguishes the Canadian system from other regulated markets is the depth of operational oversight — from seed-to-sale tracking via the Cannabis Tracking System (CTS), to mandatory SOPs, quality management systems, and Health Canada's power to inspect without meaningful notice.

The CTS requirement is non-negotiable: every gram of cannabis produced, transferred, sold, or destroyed must be tracked and reported monthly. CTS discrepancies are one of the most common triggers for compliance actions.

**The Post-Legalization Compliance Landscape**

The CannTrust scandal — in which senior executives misrepresented licensed and unlicensed grow rooms to Health Canada — resulted in criminal fraud charges and the suspension of the company's licences. The message to industry was clear: regulatory compliance is not administrative box-checking. It is a matter of legal exposure for individuals, not just corporations.

Health Canada has increased inspection frequency and remote audit capacity since 2020. Licence conditions are increasingly specific. Micro-licence holders who scaled operations beyond their approved footprint have faced licence suspension.

**Provincial Complexity**

Retail compliance adds a second regulatory layer. Each province has its own licensing authority — AGCO in Ontario, BCLDB in BC, AGLC in Alberta. Requirements for age verification training, store design, and inventory systems are provincial-level obligations layered on top of federal requirements.

The removal of store count caps in Ontario and BC has driven retail competition to levels that squeeze margins — making compliance a competitive advantage, not just a legal requirement. Stores that lose their licence for violations cannot recover their market position.

**What Good Compliance Looks Like in 2026**

The most successful cannabis businesses treat compliance as a strategic function:

- Dedicated compliance officer or manager with regulatory expertise
- Real-time CTS reporting discipline, not monthly catch-ups
- SOP library that is living (updated) not static (filed and forgotten)
- Legal counsel on retainer with cannabis regulatory experience
- Annual internal audit against Health Canada compliance expectations
- Board-level awareness of regulatory risk — D&O insurance that reflects it

The cannabis industry's compliance burden will only increase as Health Canada matures its oversight and enforcement. The organizations that build compliance culture from day one will outlast those that treat it as a cost centre.

## LinkedIn Article 2: Veterans Affairs Cannabis Coverage

**Title:** Veterans Affairs Cannabis Coverage: A Policy Analysis

**Body:**

Veterans Affairs Canada's cannabis reimbursement policy has been one of the most politically and medically contested aspects of Canada's cannabis framework since medical cannabis gained broader visibility in the early 2010s.

**The Origins of VAC Cannabis Coverage**

VAC began covering medical cannabis for veterans as an exceptional care benefit around 2008 — well before recreational legalization. At its peak, VAC covered up to 10 grams per day of dried cannabis, making Canada's program one of the most generous veteran cannabis policies in the world.

The program grew substantially as awareness spread through veteran communities, particularly among those with PTSD, chronic pain, traumatic brain injury, and treatment-resistant mental health conditions. By the mid-2010s, cannabis had become one of VAC's largest pharmaceutical expenditures.

**The 2016 Policy Change**

Under public and fiscal pressure, the government reduced the maximum reimbursable daily amount from 10g/day to 3g/day in November 2016. The move generated significant backlash from veteran advocacy organizations, who argued that the change was driven by cost concerns rather than medical evidence.

Individual veterans whose medical needs exceeded 3g/day were left seeking solutions — either paying out of pocket for excess amounts, appealing to VAC's medical review process, or adjusting their treatment approach.

**The Current Framework (2026)**

The 3g/day limit remains in effect as of 2026. Key policy parameters:

- Maximum 3g dried cannabis equivalent per day
- Requires medical document from physician or nurse practitioner
- Products must be purchased from federally licensed sellers
- Reimbursement processed through VAC exceptional care benefits
- Appeals available for medical necessity arguments

**The Medical Evidence Context**

The policy debate around VAC cannabis coverage reflects broader tensions in cannabis medicine: the evidence base for cannabis in PTSD, chronic pain, and TBI has grown substantially since 2016 but remains characterized by small study sizes and methodological heterogeneity. Veterans advocates argue the policy should lead evidence; fiscal policy advocates argue the reverse.

The emerging field of veteran-focused cannabis research — including Canadian Armed Forces member studies — continues to develop. Policy is likely to be revisited as that evidence matures.

**Implications for Policy Analysts**

VAC cannabis coverage is a case study in the tension between: (1) individual patient authorization by a healthcare provider, (2) government benefit policy, and (3) evolving evidence standards. The question of who decides — the doctor or the benefits administrator — is unresolved and has significant implications for veterans' access to care.

## LinkedIn Article 3: Constitutional Challenges in Cannabis Law

**Title:** Constitutional Challenges in Cannabis Law: From Parker to Smith

**Body:**

Canada's medical cannabis framework did not emerge from legislative vision — it was forced into existence by courts applying the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms to individual cases. Understanding this lineage is essential for anyone working in cannabis law, policy, or advocacy.

**R v Parker (2000): The Foundation**

Terrance Parker suffered from severe epilepsy. Cannabis controlled his seizures when conventional medications failed. When charged with possession and cultivation, Parker's defence counsel raised a constitutional argument: the complete prohibition on cannabis violated his rights under s.7 of the Charter — the right to life, liberty, and security of the person.

The Ontario Court of Appeal agreed. The court found that Parker was forced to choose between his liberty (comply with the law, suffer uncontrolled seizures) and his life/security (use cannabis, face criminal prosecution). This dilemma was a violation of s.7 that was not justified under s.1.

The federal government had to respond. The *Marihuana Medical Access Regulations* (MMAR) were enacted in 2001.

**Hitzig v Canada (2003): Closing the Broken System**

The MMAR was poorly designed. Authorized producers were meant to supply patients, but obtaining a supply licence was nearly impossible in practice. The Ontario Superior Court of Justice found that the system was constitutionally defective — a right without meaningful access is not a right at all.

*Hitzig* forced the government to allow patients to designate personal growers, creating the first real personal production rights in Canadian medical cannabis.

**Allard v Canada (2016): Defending Personal Production**

When the MMPR (2013) eliminated personal production in favour of a commercial LP model, patients challenged this as unconstitutional. The Federal Court agreed: the restriction to commercial LP-only supply forced patients into a more expensive and less accessible system, impairing their liberty without adequate justification.

The ACMPR was created in response, restoring personal production rights.

**R v Smith (2015): Expanding Permitted Forms**

The restriction of medical cannabis to dried flower only was challenged by Owen Smith. A unanimous Supreme Court of Canada found that restricting patients to smoking cannabis — when other forms were safer and more effective for many conditions — was an arbitrary violation of s.7.

This decision directly enabled the range of medical cannabis products available today: oils, capsules, topicals, and ultimately the Cannabis 2.0 edibles and extract categories.

**The Pattern**

Each of these cases follows a recognizable pattern: an individual faces real medical or liberty consequences from a legal restriction. Charter litigation identifies the constitutional flaw. Courts strike down the restriction. Government is forced to respond with policy change.

The lesson for cannabis policy advocates: constitutional litigation is not a last resort — it is often the only mechanism by which entrenched policy positions shift.

## LinkedIn Article 4: Cannabis in the Workplace

**Title:** Cannabis in the Workplace: Human Rights and Accommodation Law

**Body:**

For Canadian employers, the legalization of cannabis has created a new frontier in employment law. The intersection of human rights obligations, occupational health and safety duties, and the realities of a legally available substance requires careful navigation.

**The Core Human Rights Principle**

Medical cannabis users are generally entitled to accommodation in the workplace under human rights legislation across Canadian jurisdictions. This protection does not flow from the substance — it flows from the underlying medical condition for which cannabis has been authorized.

A worker using cannabis for PTSD, chronic pain, or another recognized medical condition has a disability that triggers the employer's duty to accommodate. The accommodation obligation extends to the point of undue hardship — a high bar requiring real, demonstrable hardship, not mere inconvenience.

**Safety-Sensitive Positions: The Clearest Framework**

Where impairment creates genuine safety risk — heavy equipment operators, pipeline workers, pilots, certain healthcare providers — zero-tolerance policies have been upheld by arbitrators as defensible. But three conditions must be met:

1. The safety-sensitive nature of the position must be genuine and documented
2. The policy must be clearly communicated before it is enforced
3. The policy must be consistently applied — selective enforcement undermines it

In *Stewart v Elk Valley Coal Corporation* (SCC 2017), the Supreme Court upheld termination of an employee who failed to disclose pre-existing drug use before a workplace incident. The duty to disclose a dependency in a safety-sensitive role was found to be a reasonable condition of accommodation.

**Drug Testing: Where Canadian Law Diverges From the US**

Canadian employment law does not permit random drug testing in most circumstances — a significant departure from US practice. The generally accepted framework:

- **Pre-employment testing:** Permissible only where demonstrated safety-sensitive job requirement; cannot screen out medical cannabis users without engaging in accommodation process
- **Post-incident testing:** More defensible where impairment is a plausible contributing factor
- **Reasonable cause testing:** Permissible where objective observable indicators of impairment are documented
- **Random testing:** Generally impermissible except in very limited, demonstrated high-risk contexts (*Irving Pulp & Paper* SCC 2013)

**Best Practices for Employers**

Every employer with employees in Canada — cannabis industry or otherwise — should have:

1. A written Workplace Substance Use Policy addressing cannabis specifically
2. A documented Medical Accommodation Process for employees requesting cannabis accommodation
3. Training for supervisors on recognizing impairment indicators (not drug testing — behavioural observation)
4. Legal review of the policy by employment counsel in the applicable province
5. Clear communication to all employees before the policy is enforced

The employer who acts punitively before engaging in the accommodation process faces human rights complaints. The employer who has no policy faces the equally bad outcome of inconsistency.

**The Bottom Line**

Cannabis in the workplace is a human rights issue, a safety issue, and an employment law issue simultaneously. The employers who navigate it best are those who treat it as all three — with consistent, documented, good-faith processes.

## LinkedIn Article 5: ACMPR at 10 Years

**Title:** The ACMPR at 10 Years: Access, Equity, and the Future of Medical Cannabis in Canada

**Body:**

The Access to Cannabis for Medical Purposes Regulations (ACMPR) came into force in August 2016, replacing the Marihuana for Medical Purposes Regulations (MMPR) after the Federal Court's decision in *Allard v. Canada*. A decade on, what has Canada's medical cannabis system delivered — and where does it fall short?

**What the ACMPR Got Right**

The ACMPR restored personal production rights that the MMPR had eliminated. For patients with limited income, the ability to grow their own cannabis — within authorized limits — democratized access in a meaningful way. Growing your own at a fraction of LP retail prices can mean the difference between affordable medicine and out-of-pocket expenses patients cannot sustain.

The framework also enabled a commercial LP market to mature. The LPs that emerged under the MMPR/ACMPR built the production and quality infrastructure that eventually supported recreational legalization in 2018. From a public health perspective, the shift from unregulated sources to tested, labelled, potency-verified products was a significant advance.

**Where the System Falls Short**

*Access gaps:* Rural and remote Canadians — particularly First Nations communities — face significant barriers to accessing both healthcare providers willing to authorize medical cannabis and reliable mail-order delivery from LPs.

*Cost barriers:* Medical cannabis is not covered by provincial health insurance in any province. VAC coverage assists veterans. Some private insurers have expanded coverage. But the majority of medical cannabis patients pay entirely out of pocket, often $100–$300/month or more. For patients on fixed incomes or disability benefits, this is prohibitive.

*The healthcare provider gap:* Many physicians remain reluctant to authorize medical cannabis — citing lack of training, discomfort with the evidence base, or institutional discouragement. Nurse practitioners increasingly fill this gap, but training in cannabis medicine remains inconsistent across professional programs.

*Two-tier system:* Medical patients can possess more, grow their own, and access products more directly — but these advantages come with administrative burden. The registration and renewal process is not frictionless.

**The Recreational Legalization Effect**

One intended benefit of recreational legalization was reducing stigma around cannabis use generally, including medical use. This has materialized to some degree. Simultaneously, some observers argue that the convenience of recreational retail has drawn some medical patients out of the medical system — accepting lower possession limits in exchange for no administrative overhead.

**The Next Decade**

The future of medical cannabis in Canada likely involves:

- Expanded pharmacist role in dispensing and counselling
- Insurance coverage expansion as evidence base strengthens
- Pharmaceutical-grade clinical products for specific indications (building on Sativex/Epidyolex models)
- Integration of cannabis medicine into mainstream medical education
- Evidence-based revision of authorized daily quantity guidelines

The ACMPR framework was never designed to be permanent — it was a constitutional response to a court decision. The question for Canada's second decade of regulated medical cannabis is whether policy will evolve proactively to address access and equity gaps, or continue to wait for litigation to force the next reform.

## Script 3: "THC vs CBD: What the Science Actually Says"
**Runtime:** 4–5 minutes | **Presenter:** Derek Francisco (Doc Weedlaw) | **Channel:** CCLDR.NET

**[INTRO — 0:00–0:30]**

*(On camera)*

THC gets you high. CBD doesn't. That's all you know — right?

⚖ Education only — not medical advice.

---

**[THE ECS — 0:30–1:30]**

*(Animated graphic: human body outline, CB1 (brain, CNS) and CB2 (immune system, peripheral) receptor locations)*

Your body has a system built specifically to interact with cannabis compounds. It's called the Endocannabinoid System — the ECS.

The ECS consists of three components:

**1. Receptors** — CB1, primarily in the brain and central nervous system. CB2, primarily in the immune system and peripheral tissues. Plus newer identified receptors like GPR55 and TRP channels.

**2. Endocannabinoids** — your body produces its own cannabinoid-like molecules. The most studied: anandamide — sometimes called the "bliss molecule" — and 2-AG (2-arachidonoylglycerol). These bind to CB1 and CB2 just like THC does.

**3. Enzymes** — FAAH breaks down anandamide. MAGL breaks down 2-AG. These regulate how long your endocannabinoids stay active.

The ECS was only discovered in the early 1990s. Before that, we didn't know why THC did what it did. Cannabis research led to one of the most significant discoveries in modern neuroscience.

---

**[THC — 1:30–2:30]**

*(Graphic: THC molecule, CB1 receptor binding animation)*

THC — delta-9-tetrahydrocannabinol — is a partial agonist at CB1 receptors. That means it binds to CB1 and activates it — mimicking what anandamide does, but more powerfully and for much longer.

That CB1 activation in the brain is what produces:
- Euphoria
- Altered time perception
- Increased appetite ("the munchies")
- Pain relief
- Anti-nausea effects
- At higher doses or in vulnerable individuals: anxiety, paranoia, and perceptual distortions

THC is also psychomotor-impairing — it affects reaction time, coordination, and cognitive processing. That's why driving after cannabis use is legally and genuinely impaired driving.

Medical applications with evidence: chronic pain, chemotherapy-induced nausea, appetite stimulation in wasting conditions, and certain neurological applications.

---

**[CBD — 2:30–3:30]**

*(Graphic: CBD molecule, multiple receptor interaction points)*

CBD — cannabidiol — works very differently. It does not bind strongly to CB1 receptors — which is why it doesn't produce intoxication.

Instead, CBD works through multiple mechanisms:

- **CB1/CB2 modulation** — allosteric modulation, changing how other molecules interact with these receptors
- **5-HT1A receptors** — serotonin pathway involvement, relevant to its anxiolytic effects
- **TRP channels** — involved in pain signaling
- **GPR55** — sometimes called the "third cannabinoid receptor"
- **FAAH inhibition** — CBD may inhibit the enzyme that breaks down anandamide, effectively increasing your own endocannabinoid activity

Medical applications with the strongest evidence: CBD (as Epidiolex/Epidyolex) is the first cannabis-derived pharmaceutical approved for childhood epilepsy syndromes — Dravet syndrome and Lennox-Gastaut syndrome. The evidence there is compelling and FDA/Health Canada approved.

For anxiety and sleep — the evidence is promising but still maturing. Don't be misled by marketing; check the actual research.

---

**[WHY THE RATIO MATTERS — 3:30–4:15]**

*(Graphic: THC:CBD ratio spectrum)*

The ratio of THC to CBD in a product significantly affects the experience — particularly for new users.

CBD appears to modulate THC's effects at CB1 receptors. Higher CBD ratios are associated with less anxiety and paranoia from THC. This is why 1:1 products or high-CBD strains are often recommended for those sensitive to THC's intoxicating effects.

For medical patients: the ratio matters enormously. Pain? Often higher THC. Anxiety? Often higher CBD or balanced. Epilepsy? Specific CBD formulations. Sleep? High THC with myrcene-forward terpene profile for many patients.

The "it's all just weed" framing is scientifically inadequate. Different products, ratios, and terpene profiles produce meaningfully different effects. That's not marketing — that's pharmacology.
