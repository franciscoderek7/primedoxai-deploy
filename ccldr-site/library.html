R. v. Parker
Landmark
2000 CanLII 5762 (ON CA) — Ontario Court of Appeal

Struck down the criminal prohibition on marijuana possession as unconstitutional under s.7 of the Charter. Established that a person has the constitutional right to possess cannabis for medical purposes when necessary to preserve life.

s.7 CharterMedical necessityCriminal lawEpilepsy
Key Ratio
Marijuana prohibition violates s.7 of the Charter (right to life, liberty, and security of the person) when applied to individuals who require cannabis for medical purposes. The Court held the government must provide a constitutionally valid exemption for medical use or the possession prohibition is invalid.
Impact
Led directly to the creation of the Marihuana Medical Access Regulations (MMAR) in 2001. The foundational case for all subsequent medical cannabis rights litigation in Canada.
Doc WeedLaw's Take
Parker is your constitution. When the Crown argues possession is always criminal, Parker says: not when life and health are at stake. This case gave us MMAR, then ACMPR, then the Cannabis Act framework. Know it cold.
Allard v. Canada (Allard #1 & #2)
Landmark
2016 FC 236 — Federal Court of Canada

Struck down MMPR (Marihuana for Medical Purposes Regulations) as unconstitutional. Patients cannot be forced to obtain cannabis only from licensed producers — they have the constitutional right to grow their own medicine.

s.7 CharterMMPR struck downHome cultivationMedical access
Key Ratio
The MMPR violated patients' s.7 rights by prohibiting them from growing their own medical cannabis and forcing them into a more expensive commercial market. The court found the regulations impeded reasonable access to medicine.
Impact
Directly led to the ACMPR (Access to Cannabis for Medical Purposes Regulations) which restored the right to home cultivation for medical patients. Constitutional protection for growing your own medicine.
R. v. Smith
Landmark
2015 SCC 34 — Supreme Court of Canada

Struck down the restrictions on cannabis derivative products. The government cannot allow raw cannabis for medical patients while prohibiting cannabis oils, edibles, and other forms — that violates s.7 Charter rights.

s.7 CharterCannabis derivativesExtractsSCC
Key Ratio
The prohibition on non-dried forms of medical cannabis was unconstitutional. Patients cannot be forced to smoke their medicine when other forms are medically preferable. The right to access medical cannabis extends to all forms of the substance.
Impact
Led to the legalization of cannabis oils, extracts, and edibles for medical purposes. Foundation for Cannabis 2.0 (extracts, edibles, topicals legalized in 2019).
R. v. Grant
Charter
2009 SCC 32 — Supreme Court of Canada

Establishes the three-part test for determining whether evidence obtained through a Charter breach should be excluded under s.24(2). The foundational case for Charter remedies.

s.24(2) exclusionEvidenceCharter remedies3-part test
The Grant Test (3 Branches)
1. Seriousness of the Charter-infringing conduct (was it deliberate, systemic, or inadvertent?)
2. Impact of the breach on the Charter-protected interests of the accused (direct? peripheral?)
3. Society's interest in adjudication on the merits (balancing act)
Doc WeedLaw's Take
Grant is the key that unlocks s.24(2). If police violated your Charter rights to get evidence, Grant is how you argue exclusion. Memorize all three branches. Apply each one to your specific facts. A well-argued Grant analysis can exclude the Crown's entire case.
R. v. Hitzig
Charter
2003 CanLII 42488 (ON CA)

Struck down MMAR provisions that prohibited designated persons from selling cannabis to patients. The court held that patients must have a reasonable means of obtaining their medicine — the exemption cannot be illusory.

MMARMedical accessDesignated personss.7 Charter
Key Ratio
A constitutional exemption for medical cannabis that cannot be practically exercised is no exemption at all. The government must ensure patients can actually obtain their medicine, not just theoretically access it.
R. v. Mernagh
Landmark
2011 ONSC 2121, aff'd 2013 ONCA 67

MMAR struck down at Superior Court level — the regime was so impractical that it was constitutionally invalid. On appeal, the finding was narrowed but the principle of practical access reinforced.

MMARPractical accesss.7 CharterOntario
Key Ratio
The MMAR's physician approval requirement was constitutionally deficient because most Canadian doctors would not sign the required forms, making the exemption practically unavailable to those who needed it most.
R. v. Saeed
Charter
2016 SCC 24 — Supreme Court of Canada

Clarified when a search incident to arrest is constitutionally permissible. The scope of a warrantless search must be tailored to the arrest — cannot be a blanket authorization to search anything and everything.

R. v. Ward
Charter
2012 ONCA 660 — Ontario Court of Appeal

Important case on damages for Charter breaches. Police can be ordered to pay compensation to individuals whose Charter rights were violated — personal consequences for Charter violations.

s.24(1) damagesCharter breachPolice accountabilityCompensation
Key Ratio
Courts can award damages under s.24(1) of the Charter for government violations of constitutional rights. Ward established the framework: functional justification, good governance concerns, and consistency with the Charter's remedial purposes all factor in.
Doc WeedLaw's Take
Ward is your civil remedy. If police violated your Charter rights and exclusion of evidence isn't enough, you can sue for damages. Derek Francisco uses this framework in ongoing litigation against the Crown.
R. v. Stairs
Charter
2022 SCC 11 — Supreme Court of Canada

Limits the scope of search incident to arrest — police cannot conduct a full warrantless search of an entire residence upon arrest. Recent and important for cannabis possession cases where police search homes.
