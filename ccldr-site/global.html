Cannabis Around the World

How Different Nations Approach Cannabis Policy — From Federal Legalization to Mandatory Death Penalties

International Law

The UN Treaty Framework

Cannabis prohibition did not arise organically — it was shaped by international treaty obligations dating back to the early 20th century. Understanding these treaties is foundational to understanding cannabis law globally.

1961 Single Convention on Narcotic Drugs

The primary international treaty that placed cannabis under Schedule I (high abuse potential, no accepted medical use) and Schedule IV (most dangerous, most restrictive). Nations party to this treaty were obligated to criminalize and control cannabis at the federal level. Canada became a signatory.

Schedule I = high abuse potential. Schedule IV (former) = most dangerous, no medicinal value — the most restrictive classification available.

December 2, 2020 — Historic UN Vote

The United Nations Commission on Narcotic Drugs (CND) voted to remove cannabis from Schedule IV of the 1961 Single Convention — the first significant international reclassification since the treaty was adopted.

Final Vote:
27 YES 25 NO 1 ABSTAIN
Canada: YES — Supported reclassification
United States: YES — Reversed historical opposition
Russia: NO — Opposed
China: NO — Opposed

Why Schedule IV Removal Matters

Being listed in Schedule IV prevented nations from acknowledging any medical value. Removal from Schedule IV means member states have more flexibility to develop medical programs without directly violating treaty obligations. Cannabis remains in Schedule I, meaning international control continues — but the door to medical legitimacy was formally opened.

Other Relevant Treaties

1971 Convention on Psychotropic Substances — Addresses THC and synthetic cannabinoids. Separately from the 1961 treaty.

1988 UN Drug Convention — Required criminalization of drug trafficking. Most permissive nations still maintain trafficking laws.

Canada argued its Cannabis Act balances treaty obligations with domestic constitutional rights and public health priorities.

Critical Warning

International Travel Warning

⚠ NEVER Transport Cannabis Across Any International Border

  • ⚠ US BORDER: Declaring past cannabis use — even legal use in Canada — can result in a potential lifetime bar from US admissibility. CBP officers can deny entry based on admission of use. You are not required to answer questions about past legal activity, but refusal may also result in denial of entry. Consult an immigration lawyer before crossing.
  • ⚠ UNITED KINGDOM: Cannabis is classified as a Class B drug under the Misuse of Drugs Act 1971. Possession carries up to 5 years imprisonment. Supply carries up to 14 years.
  • 🚫 SINGAPORE: MANDATORY DEATH PENALTY for trafficking 500g or more of cannabis. For amounts above 28g the presumption of trafficking applies. Singapore has among the harshest drug laws on earth. There are no exceptions.
  • 🚫 UNITED ARAB EMIRATES (UAE): Zero tolerance. Trace amounts — including in bloodstream — can result in imprisonment. Dubai has arrested travelers for residues found in luggage. Even prescription medical cannabis from Canada provides no protection.
  • 🚫 JAPAN: Zero tolerance policy. Possession of even small amounts can result in years of imprisonment. Foreign nationals face deportation after serving sentences. Japan tests for cannabis more aggressively than most nations.
  • 🚫 PHILIPPINES: Death penalty risk for trafficking under the Comprehensive Dangerous Drugs Act. President Marcos has maintained aggressive anti-drug policies. Foreigners are not exempt. Possession can mean life imprisonment.
  • ⚠ INDONESIA: Mandatory minimum sentences; trafficking carries life imprisonment or death. Bali is not an exception — tourists have served multi-year sentences for small quantities.
⚠ Verify laws at your destination before you travel. Do not assume legal-at-home = legal there. Your Canadian rights end at the Canadian border. CCLDR.NET provides this information for education only — not legal advice. Consult a licensed lawyer before traveling.
Detailed Analysis

The 2020 UN Rescheduling — What It Means

The December 2, 2020 vote was the most significant international cannabis policy shift in 60 years. Here is what happened and why it matters.

1

WHO Expert Committee Recommendation (2019)

The World Health Organization's Expert Committee on Drug Dependence (ECDD) reviewed cannabis and cannabinoids for the first time in decades. They submitted six recommendations to the CND, including removing cannabis from Schedule IV of the 1961 Convention — recognizing its documented medical uses and established safety profile relative to other Schedule IV substances.

2

Delayed Vote — Political Tension

The vote was originally scheduled for March 2020 but was delayed repeatedly as member states lobbied. The US position — which had historically voted to maintain harsh scheduling — shifted dramatically when the Biden administration indicated support for rescheduling, aligning with the WHO recommendation. This was seen as a major turning point.

3

The December 2, 2020 Vote

The Commission voted 27–25 with 1 abstention to adopt Recommendation 5.4 — removing cannabis and cannabis resin from Schedule IV while retaining Schedule I listing. This was a razor-thin majority. Canada, the US, EU nations, and several Latin American countries voted yes. Russia, China, Nigeria, Pakistan, and others voted no.

4

What Changed — and What Didn't

Cannabis remains in Schedule I of the 1961 Convention — meaning international control continues. Nations still must regulate it under the treaty framework. What changed: the explicit designation as having "no medical use" was removed. Nations now have clearer international footing to develop medical programs without claiming treaty non-compliance.

5

Significance for Canada

Canada legalized recreationally in 2018 — before this vote. Canada argued it maintained the spirit of treaty obligations through its regulatory framework. The 2020 vote provided retroactive international validation. Canada's model — regulated access with age limits, taxation, and licensed producers — is now cited globally as the framework for compliant legalization.

Canadian Leadership

Canada's International Role

Canada made history on October 17, 2018, becoming the first G7 nation and the second country in the world to federally legalize recreational cannabis.

First G7 Nation to Federally Legalize Recreational Cannabis

When Canada enacted the Cannabis Act (Bill C-45) on October 17, 2018, it sent a signal to the world that a major, stable, democratic nation could create a regulated adult-use market while maintaining treaty obligations and protecting public health. Uruguay had legalized in 2013 but was not a G7 member.

Canada's framework has been studied by governments worldwide as a model for tax structure, licensed production, provincial distribution, and public health provisions including impaired driving law reform.

  • Federal Cannabis Act creates national framework; provinces control retail distribution
  • Health Canada regulates licensed producers, testing, and packaging standards
  • ACMPR (Access to Cannabis for Medical Purposes Regulations) governs patient access
  • 30-gram public possession limit; 4-plant home cultivation permitted nationally
  • Canada has generated over $6 billion in cannabis tax revenue since 2018
  • Canadian researchers lead global cannabis science including endocannabinoid studies
  • Canada exports medical cannabis to Germany, Israel, UK, and other markets
  • The Cannabis Act is reviewed every 3 years by the Senate Standing Committee
