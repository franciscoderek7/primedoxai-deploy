Harm Reduction Education

Safe, Informed Cannabis Use

Harm reduction education for all experience levels — from first-time users to experienced consumers. Evidence-based, honest, and judgment-free.

Section 01

First-Time User Guide

Going in prepared makes all the difference. Follow this checklist before, during, and after your first experience.

Before You Start
  • Choose a safe, comfortable, familiar setting — your home is ideal
  • Have a trusted, sober friend present for your first experience
  • Do not mix with alcohol
  • Clear your schedule for 4–6 hours
  • Have water and snacks available
  • Know where you are and how to get help if needed
Choosing Your First Product
  • Start with CBD-dominant or balanced (CBD:THC 1:1 or higher CBD)
  • Avoid concentrates, high-THC extracts, or edibles for your first experience
  • Dried flower / vaporizer: easiest to titrate (control dosage)
  • Edibles: NOT recommended for first time — onset delay causes overconsumption
Starting Your Session

Take 1–2 small puffs / drops / mg — then WAIT 15 minutes before assessing. Do not redose early.

If It Goes Wrong — "Greening Out"
  • Stay calm — no one has ever died from a cannabis overdose
  • Lie down in recovery position if nauseous
  • Cold water, deep breathing — CBD can reduce THC effects
  • Call a trusted friend, not 911 (unless medical emergency — e.g. heart condition, loss of consciousness)
  • Remind yourself: "The high will pass" — effects typically subside within 2–4 hours
Section 02

Harm Reduction Principles

Universal principles supported by public health evidence. Apply these regardless of your experience level.

⚕ Core Harm Reduction Principles
  • Start low, go slow — this is the universal principle across all methods and products
  • Know your source: legal market only — no pesticides, tested for potency and contaminants
  • Frequency matters: daily use develops tolerance and dependence faster than occasional use
  • Mental health monitoring: if you notice increased anxiety, paranoia, or low mood — stop and reassess
  • Set and Setting: your environment and mindset significantly affect your experience
  • Storage: keep away from children and pets — locked box in a dark, cool location
  • Don't drive: impairment can last 4–6 hours after smoking, longer with edibles
  • Tolerance breaks: periodic abstinence resets sensitivity and reduces dependence risk
Section 03

Drug Interactions

Cannabis interacts with many common medications. This is not a complete list — always consult your pharmacist.

⚠ CRITICAL — Tell Your Doctor and Pharmacist

ALWAYS tell your doctor and pharmacist about cannabis use. Many interactions are dose-dependent and may require medication adjustments. Failure to disclose can result in serious adverse events.

Section 04

When Not to Use Cannabis

Evidence-based contraindications. These are not moral judgments — they are health risk categories.

⚠ Pregnancy

THC crosses the placental barrier and blood-brain barrier. Evidence links prenatal cannabis exposure to fetal neurodevelopmental effects. No safe dose has been established.

⚠ Breastfeeding

THC is excreted in breast milk and may accumulate in infant tissues. Infants have immature metabolic systems. Avoid during breastfeeding.

⚠ Under Age 25

The developing brain (maturation completes ~age 25) is more vulnerable to cannabis effects, particularly high-THC products. Risk of lasting cognitive effects is higher.

⚠ Psychosis / Schizophrenia History

Personal or family history of psychosis or schizophrenia represents significant risk amplification. High-THC cannabis can precipitate or worsen psychotic episodes.

⚠ Severe Cardiovascular Disease

THC elevates heart rate (tachycardia). Can trigger cardiac events in those with existing heart conditions. Consult your cardiologist before any use.

⚠ Before Driving

Legal impairment limit (2 ng/mL blood THC) can be reached with recreational doses. Impairment lasts longer than subjective "high." Do not drive for 4–6+ hours after use.

⚠ Operating Heavy Machinery

Impaired reaction time, coordination, and judgment pose serious safety risks in any operating-machinery context, regardless of tolerance level.

⚠ With Alcohol

Synergistic (multiplied, not merely additive) impairment. "Cross-fading" significantly increases risk of adverse reactions, nausea, and impairment beyond expectations.

⚠ Unknown Mental Health Conditions

First episodes of psychosis are sometimes cannabis-precipitated. If you have never been assessed for a mental health condition and have risk factors, proceed with extreme caution or abstain.

Section 05

Responsible Storage

Proper storage protects potency, prevents accidental exposure, and is required by law in many contexts.

🔒
Lock Box or Safe

Strongly recommended; required where children are present. Childproof locks are a legal requirement for commercial cannabis packaging in Canada.

🌡
Temperature & Humidity

Optimal: 15–20°C, low humidity (55–62% RH). Heat degrades cannabinoids; moisture promotes mold. Avoid direct sunlight.

🌑
Light Exposure

UV light degrades THC and terpenes faster than any other factor. Store in opaque, airtight containers in dark locations.

🐕
Pets — Serious Risk

Dogs are especially attracted to cannabis edibles. Cannabis toxicity in pets is serious — causes lethargy, vomiting, tremors. Contact your vet immediately if exposure is suspected.

🗑
Disposal

Follow local municipality guidelines for cannabis disposal. Do not flush — cannabis compounds can affect aquatic environments. Mix with undesirable material (coffee grounds) and bag.

📦
Original Packaging

Keep cannabis in its original legal packaging when possible — it includes potency information, batch numbers, and is child-resistant by regulation.

Section 06

Recognizing Problematic Use

Cannabis Use Disorder (CUD) is real, diagnosable, and treatable. Recognizing the signs is the first step — without judgment.

DSM-5 Indicators

Clinically significant impairment with 2+ of the following in a 12-month period:

  • Using more than intended; difficulty cutting down
  • Craving; spending significant time obtaining, using, or recovering
  • Continued use despite relationship or work problems
  • Giving up important activities to use
  • Tolerance — needing more for the same effect
  • Withdrawal: irritability, insomnia, anxiety, decreased appetite
  • Using in physically hazardous situations
  • Continued use despite worsening physical or mental health

A Note on Judgment

Cannabis dependence exists on a spectrum and affects a minority of users — approximately 9% of people who ever try cannabis, rising to ~17% of those who start in adolescence (NIDA). If you or someone you know is struggling, seeking help is a sign of self-awareness, not weakness. Addiction is a medical condition, not a moral failing.
