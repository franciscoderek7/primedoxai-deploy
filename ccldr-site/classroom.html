Lesson 1: Section 7 — Life, Liberty, and Security of the Person

What s.7 Says: "Everyone has the right to life, liberty and security of the person and the right not to be deprived thereof except in accordance with the principles of fundamental justice."

Why It Matters to You: Section 7 is the backbone of cannabis Charter challenges. In R. v. Parker (2000), the Ontario Court of Appeal struck down the possession prohibition as a violation of s.7 — the first time a court found that denying medical cannabis access violated a person's constitutional right to health and security. If the state threatens your life or health through a law, that law must comply with fundamental justice.

Fundamental Justice: The Supreme Court has defined principles of fundamental justice to include: laws must not be arbitrary, laws must not be overbroad, and laws must not have grossly disproportionate effects. These three principles are your tools when challenging cannabis law.

Invoking s.7: At your hearing, your lawyer or you (self-represented) can argue: "Your Honour, the Crown's position deprives my client of liberty through arrest and prosecution. That deprivation must accord with the principles of fundamental justice — specifically, the law as applied here is overbroad and grossly disproportionate." That framing opens the constitutional door.

Quick Reference — Know These Cold
Charter s.8 — Search & Seizure
Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms, 1982
  • "Everyone has the right to be secure against unreasonable search or seizure."
  • Police must have reasonable and probable grounds before searching you, your home, or your vehicle.
  • A warrant is required in most circumstances — warrantless searches are presumptively unreasonable.
  • Reasonable expectation of privacy (REP) is the threshold question — established in Hunter v. Southam (1984).
  • Evidence from an unreasonable search can be excluded under s.24(2).
Charter s.10(b) — Right to Counsel
Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms, 1982
  • On arrest or detention, you have the right to retain and instruct counsel without delay.
  • Police must inform you of this right (informational component) and facilitate it (implementational component).
  • Once invoked: all questioning must stop until you have spoken with a lawyer.
  • You have the right to Legal Aid duty counsel — available 24/7 at no cost.
  • Say clearly: "I want to speak to a lawyer" — then say nothing else.
R. v. Grant — The 3-Part Exclusion Test
2009 SCC 32 — Supreme Court of Canada
  • Step 1: Seriousness of the Charter-infringing conduct — was it deliberate or egregious?
  • Step 2: Impact on the accused's Charter-protected interests — how serious was the breach?
  • Step 3: Society's interest in adjudication on the merits — would exclusion bring the justice system into disrepute?
  • Courts balance all three factors holistically — there is no automatic exclusion rule.
  • The more serious the breach, the stronger the case for exclusion under s.24(2).
