FP-2026-003  ·  Majority Opinion  ·  Filed June 2026
Medical Cannabis Access Standards — Minimum Access Thresholds
5 — 2   Majority

Written by Justice Compassion  ·  Concurring: Justice Liberty, Justice Privacy, Justice Equity, Justice Commerce  ·  Dissenting: Justice Order (with reasons)

The question before this Court is whether the Access to Cannabis for Medical Purposes Regulations, and their successor instruments, satisfy the constitutional minimum established under Article II of the WeedLaw Constitution and s.7 of the Charter as interpreted in Parker and Allard. The answer is no — not in their present form. Where a regulatory scheme requires a patient to navigate a system so burdensome, so costly, and so stigmatized that meaningful access remains theoretical rather than actual, the scheme fails the constitutional test. The right to medicine is not merely the right to apply for medicine. It is the right to receive it, in a form that works, at a price that does not punish poverty, through a process that does not re-traumatize. The state may regulate the manner of access; it may not regulate access out of existence. This Court holds that minimum access thresholds must be defined by reference to what a person of average means can actually obtain — not by what regulations nominally permit. The regulations are struck, with a 12-month suspension of declaration to permit remedial re-drafting consistent with this Opinion.

Concurring: Justice Liberty, Justice Privacy, Justice Equity, Justice Commerce all concur in the result. Justice Commerce writes separately to note that a well-functioning legal market would make minimum access thresholds unnecessary by making cannabis medicine readily available through competitive commercial channels.

Dissenting: Justice Order (joined by none): Parliament's statutory scheme represents a reasonable accommodation of competing interests. The majority's re-drafting order exceeds the judicial function. Courts declare; legislatures remedy. I would have issued a declaration only and left timing entirely to Parliament's discretion.

FP-2026-001 (Interim)  ·  Interim Ruling  ·  Filed May 2026
Re: Right to Cultivate — Interim Ruling on Permissible Search Scope
6 — 1   Interim Order

Written by Justice Privacy  ·  Concurring: All justices except Justice Order

While the main matter in FP-2026-001 remains in deliberation, this Court issues an interim ruling on the narrow question of permissible search scope arising from the cultivation context: enforcement agents who enter a private dwelling pursuant to a general warrant do not thereby acquire authority to search cultivation areas not specified in the warrant instrument. The plain text of Article III and Charter s.8 is unambiguous. Specificity is not a technicality — it is the constitutional minimum. Any evidence of cannabis cultivation obtained by exceeding the stated scope of a search warrant is excluded under Article VII of this Constitution. The exclusionary rule requires no case-by-case balancing when the violation involves warrantless entry into a constitutionally protected space. Evidence of such a violation is excluded, period. This interim ruling applies immediately to all matters pending before any court that chooses to recognize the jurisdiction of the Francisco Protocol.

Dissenting (Justice Order): Interim rulings on evidentiary questions should await full deliberation on the main matter. I dissent from the procedural urgency, not the underlying principle. Parliament has the authority to define the contours of warrant scope, and courts should engage that statutory text before declaring constitutional violations.

Advisory Opinion  ·  All Seven Justices  ·  Filed June 2026
On the Constitutionality of Minimum Quantity Restrictions in Cannabis Legislation
7 — 0   Unanimous

Written by Justice Prime (Chief Justice)  ·  For a Unanimous Court

The Court addresses, by advisory opinion, the constitutional status of quantity restrictions that fall below the threshold of practical access. We are unanimous. Statutory provisions that permit possession of cannabis in quantities insufficient to meet a patient's documented medical needs are unconstitutional on their face. The quantity a person may lawfully possess must, at minimum, equal the quantity they demonstrably require. To criminalize possession of amounts a person needs for survival — whether for pain management, seizure control, or mental health stability — is to criminalize survival itself. No legislature may, through the mechanism of a quantity limit, effectively nullify a constitutional right that the judiciary has recognized as grounded in the right to life and security. This Opinion is advisory, but it carries the full deliberative weight of a unanimous Court. We invite all legislative bodies with jurisdiction over cannabis quantity restrictions to review their statutes in light of this Opinion immediately, and without waiting for individual constitutional challenges to work their way through the courts at the cost of individual rights-holders.

All concurring: Justice Liberty, Justice Order, Justice Compassion, Justice Commerce, Justice Privacy, Justice Equity — unanimous without qualification. This is the rare case in which every philosophical approach to constitutional interpretation — textual, purposive, structural, and equitable — converges on the same result. Minimum quantity restrictions that make practical access impossible are unconstitutional. Full stop.
