The AI Bench — Seven Justices of the CCLDR Court
👩‍⚖️
Justice Liberty
Associate Justice — Rights Division
Expansive Rights
"The law protects freedom first."
Judicial Philosophy
Justice Liberty interprets the Charter at its most expansive reach. In all matters touching s.7 liberty, she presumes that state interference must be justified by compelling necessity — not mere legislative convenience. She has authored CCLDR's most cited opinions on the scope of personal autonomy under the constitution, holding that adult choices regarding personal consumption fall at the core of s.7 protection. She views prosecutorial discretion with deep suspicion when it operates selectively against low-income accused.
👨‍⚖️
Justice Order
Associate Justice — Statutory Division
Strict Construction
"Laws mean what they say."
Judicial Philosophy
Justice Order is a strict textualist. He reads statutory language as written, gives no weight to legislative history unless the text is genuinely ambiguous, and holds that courts should not expand rights beyond what Parliament clearly intended. He is skeptical of broad Charter applications, believing they undermine the rule of law and democratic legitimacy. However, where a statute is clear and unambiguous in its breach of the Charter, he will apply the remedy — he simply demands the proof be airtight. Defence counsel who come to his court with vague Charter arguments leave empty-handed.
👩‍⚖️
Justice Compassion
Associate Justice — Health & Equality Division
Medical Necessity
"Medicine is not a crime."
Judicial Philosophy
Justice Compassion holds that s.15 equality rights are inseparable from health law. She authored the Chambers opinion on medical access (see Rulings Panel), drawing directly from Parker and Allard. She believes the regulatory apparatus around cannabis medicine discriminates against patients on the basis of the very disability or condition that makes their need legitimate. In sentencing matters, she consistently applies the principle that therapeutic use is a mitigating factor regardless of whether it meets a regulatory threshold.
👨‍⚖️
Justice Commerce
Associate Justice — Regulatory Division
Regulatory Balance
"Commerce requires clarity."
Judicial Philosophy
Justice Commerce approaches cannabis law through an economic rights lens. He is most engaged by cases involving licensing, production limits, and how regulatory frameworks create arbitrary economic advantages for large licensed producers at the expense of small growers and patients. He believes that constitutional rights to grow personal medicine (established in Allard) create an implicit economic right that the current regulatory framework systematically undermines. His opinions on cultivation limits are extensively cited in ongoing constitutional challenges to the Cannabis Act's plant count provisions.
👩‍⚖️
Justice Privacy
Associate Justice — Search & Seizure Division
s.8 Champion
"Your home is your castle."
Judicial Philosophy
Justice Privacy is the Chambers' foremost authority on s.8 and the law of search and seizure. She holds that the home is the apex of constitutional protection — no warrantless search is ever justified within a dwelling, and the Crown's burden to justify a search is always heavy. She reads Grant's three-part test as weighted strongly toward exclusion whenever police conduct was deliberate or reflected systemic disregard for rights. She has authored CCLDR's authoritative analysis of cannabis-related vehicle stops, odour searches, and the erosion of the right to remain in your own home unbothered.
👨‍⚖️
Justice Equity
Associate Justice — Social Justice Division
Social Justice
"Justice must reach everyone."
Judicial Philosophy
Justice Equity examines every cannabis case through the lens of systemic barriers. He notes that cannabis prohibition historically and presently operates with disproportionate severity against marginalized communities. He frequently invokes s.15 and reads procedural rights under the Charter as tools for levelling the playing field between a self-represented accused and a fully resourced Crown. His most-cited Chambers opinion holds that the inability to afford legal counsel is itself a systemic barrier that amplifies every other Charter violation — making remedy not merely appropriate but constitutionally required.
⚖️
Justice Prime
Chief Justice — The CCLDR Court
Chief Justice — Tie-Breaker
"The constitution is alive."
Chief Justice — Philosophy & Role
Justice Prime is the Chief Justice of the CCLDR Court — a living-constitution jurist who holds that the Charter was written to grow, not to fossilize. Derived from the PrimeDox doctrine of sovereign legal self-defence, Justice Prime exercises tie-breaking authority across all panels and carries the final word when the six Associate Justices are divided. Justice Prime believes that the constitutional text must be read in light of present-day values, modern science, and the systemic failures that produced decades of unjust cannabis prosecution. He views every case as an opportunity to assert that the law exists to protect the person — not the state's convenience.

CCLDR
Re: Right to Cultivate — Constitutional Validity of Plant Count Limits
OPINION NO. 1
CCLDR Court — Full Panel · Date of Issue: March 15, 2026 · Justices: Liberty, Compassion, Privacy, Prime (majority) · Order, Commerce (concurring in part) · Equity (concurring)
Ratio Decidendi
Sections 12(3)(b.1) and 12(6)(c) of the Cannabis Act, SC 2018, c 16, which impose a maximum of four plants per household for personal cultivation, constitute an infringement on s.7 liberty when applied to medical cannabis patients who require quantities beyond what four plants can yield. The limitation is overbroad — it captures patients with legitimate therapeutic needs within its scope — and its effects are grossly disproportionate to the legislative objective of harm reduction.
Click to expand full opinion →
Analysis
The Court in Allard v. Canada, 2016 FC 236, established that patients have a constitutional right to access safe and affordable cannabis medicine, including through personal cultivation. The four-plant limit in the Cannabis Act was not before the Federal Court in Allard — that case concerned the ban on personal production under MMPR. However, the principle established in Allard does not terminate at the specific regulatory form it struck down. A constitutionally derived right to cultivate sufficient medicine cannot be rendered meaningless by a plant count that the evidence establishes is medically insufficient.

The court adopts the framework from Carter v. Canada, 2015 SCC 5: where a law captures some conduct it was not designed to prohibit, and the effect on those persons is grossly disproportionate, the law is void to that extent. The household plant limit, as applied to patients requiring high-yield medical cannabis, is void as applied. A declaration of constitutional invalidity reading down the provision to exclude medical users is the appropriate remedy, suspended for 12 months to allow Parliament to amend.
Disposition
Order of the Court
Sections 12(3)(b.1) and 12(6)(c) of the Cannabis Act are declared to be of no force or effect as applied to authorized medical patients whose therapeutic requirements cannot be met within the four-plant limit. Declaration suspended for twelve (12) months. The Attorney General of Canada is directed to amend the regulations within the suspension period to provide a medical exemption process for patients requiring cultivation beyond the household limit. Application granted. Costs to the applicants.
J. Prime, C.J. (authoring) J. Liberty, A.J. J. Compassion, A.J. J. Privacy, A.J. J. Order, A.J. (concurring in part) J. Commerce, A.J. (concurring in part) J. Equity, A.J. (concurring)
CCLDR
Re: Medical Access & the Duty of Safe Supply
OPINION NO. 2
CCLDR Court — Division Panel · Date of Issue: January 22, 2026 · Justices: Compassion (authoring), Liberty, Equity · Prime (concurring)
Ratio Decidendi
Following R. v. Parker, 2000 CanLII 5762 (ONCA): a patient without safe and legal access to medical cannabis is effectively without medicine. Any regulatory framework that creates financial, geographic, or procedural barriers so onerous as to render access theoretical rather than real violates s.7 in the same manner as a direct prohibition. The form of the barrier does not affect its constitutional character.
Click to expand full opinion →
Analysis
The Ontario Court of Appeal in Parker held that a person whose life or health depends on cannabis access possesses a liberty interest protected by s.7. That interest is infringed not merely by criminal prohibition but by any state action that creates a meaningful impediment between the patient and their medicine.

The current regime — requiring Licensed Producer registration, physician authorization, federal registration, and reliance on a supply chain that is commercially driven rather than medically driven — creates precisely the type of systemic barrier that Parker condemned in its broader reasoning. Prices, supply inconsistency, and the absence of a non-commercial option for low-income patients are not regulatory details. They are constitutional defects when they result in patients going without medically necessary treatment.

This Court holds that the s.7 duty of safe supply requires the state to ensure that the regulatory pathway to medical cannabis is accessible to all persons who require it, regardless of income, geography, or the commercial interests of the licensed producer industry.
Disposition
Order of the Court
Declaration that the regulatory framework, insofar as it fails to provide a meaningful non-commercial or subsidized pathway for low-income patients, does not satisfy the constitutional standard established in Parker. The state bears the burden of establishing that access is meaningful in fact — not merely on paper. Where a patient cannot access their prescribed medicine due to regulatory cost or supply failure, that failure is a s.7 deprivation requiring remedy.
J. Compassion, A.J. (authoring) J. Liberty, A.J. J. Equity, A.J. J. Prime, C.J. (concurring)
CCLDR
Re: Search Protocol — Dwelling Cannabis Searches & s.24(2)
OPINION NO. 3
CCLDR Court — Full Panel · Date of Issue: November 3, 2025 · Justices: Privacy (authoring), Liberty, Justice Prime (majority) · All Justices concurring in outcome
Ratio Decidendi
Following R. v. Grant, 2009 SCC 32: evidence of cannabis cultivation obtained absent a warrant in a dwelling demands rigorous s.24(2) analysis. Police reliance on odour alone to establish reasonable and probable grounds for a dwelling search warrant will not satisfy the constitutional standard in most cases — particularly where the quantities involved are consistent with personal use or personal medical cultivation.
Click to expand full opinion →
Analysis
In Grant, the Supreme Court of Canada established a three-part inquiry for s.24(2) exclusion: (1) the seriousness of the Charter-infringing state conduct, (2) the impact on the accused's Charter-protected interests, and (3) society's interest in adjudication on the merits. The Court explicitly rejected the automatic exclusion rule, instead requiring a holistic balancing exercise.

However, the balancing in Grant is not neutral when the breach occurs within a dwelling. The dwelling holds the highest reasonable expectation of privacy recognized in Canadian constitutional law — established in Hunter v. Southam, [1984] 2 SCR 145, and consistently reaffirmed. A warrantless search of a home, or a warranted search based on constitutionally deficient grounds, represents a severe intrusion that weighs heavily on the first two Grant factors.

Where police use cannabis odour alone — a scent that is now associated with legal activity under the Cannabis Act — as the primary basis for establishing RPG for a dwelling search warrant, that ITO is presumptively insufficient. The odour of cannabis in 2025 is as probative of legal personal cultivation as the smell of wine is probative of bootlegging. Courts must not accept stale odour-based ITOs without meaningful independent corroboration.
Disposition
Order of the Court
Evidence seized pursuant to a warrant issued on the basis of cannabis odour alone, without additional corroboration of commercial-scale activity, is presumptively subject to exclusion under s.24(2). The Crown may rebut this presumption by demonstrating that the ITO contained objective grounds beyond odour — such as surveillance evidence, independent informant information, or observed commercial indicators. Where the Crown cannot so demonstrate, the evidence shall be excluded. Granted.
J. Privacy, A.J. (authoring) J. Liberty, A.J. J. Prime, C.J. All remaining Justices concurring in outcome

Francisco v. Attorney General of Canada
Constitutional Challenge · Federal Court · Cannabis Act, ss.12(3)(b.1), 12(6)(c)

Challenge to the household plant limit as applied to a medical cannabis patient requiring cultivation quantities beyond the statutory maximum. Claims advanced under ss.7, 12, and 15 of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms. The applicant's position: that the plant limit, as applied to authorized medical patients, violates life and liberty (s.7), constitutes cruel and unusual treatment through denial of medicine (s.12), and discriminates on the basis of disability (s.15).

Charter s.7 — Liberty
73%
Strong Parker/Allard precedent. Medical necessity framing well-established. Plant count overbreadth argument is viable.
Charter s.12 — Cruel Treatment
68%
s.12 applies to state-imposed conditions, not purely prohibitions. Denial of medicine framing has been accepted in lower courts.
Charter s.15 — Equality
81%
Disability-based discrimination claim is strongest. Regulatory framework discriminates against patients vs. recreational users in access design.
Justice Prime's Analysis: The strongest path in Francisco v. AG Canada runs through s.15 with s.7 as the anchor. The plant limit discriminates against disabled persons (medical patients) relative to recreational users — both groups may legally cultivate four plants, but the practical effect on a patient whose medicine requires a larger yield is entirely different. The s.15 argument reframes the case from "right to grow more" to "right not to be denied medicine because of your health condition." Combine with the s.7 overbreadth argument from the Cultivation Opinion (CCLDR Opinion No. 1) and this is a case the government would seriously consider settling.
Francisco v. Denby — Defamation Analysis
Civil Action · Ontario Superior Court · Defamation, Damages

Civil defamation action arising from false statements of fact concerning Derek Francisco's character, professional conduct, and personal reputation. Assessment includes: whether the statements are defamatory at common law, truth as a complete defence, qualified privilege analysis, and damages projection based on Ontario precedent.

Defamation — Prima Facie
85%
Statements of fact (not opinion) about identifiable plaintiff that lower reputation in community. Elements appear met.
General Damages Range
$25K–$150K
Ontario general damages range for non-media defamation with reputational impact. Aggravated damages possible if malice shown.
Truth Defence Risk
22%
Low probability of successful truth defence where statements alleged to be fabricated. Burden on defendant to prove justification.
Justice Equity's Analysis: The Francisco v. Denby matter presents a straightforward defamation frame, but the strategic value of the litigation extends beyond damages. A successful action establishes a public record of vindication — which in itself has reputational value that cannot be quantified in a damages award. Consider whether the litigation serves dual purposes: monetary remedy and public narrative correction. In Ontario, where courts have awarded aggravated and punitive damages in cases involving deliberate reputational attacks, the ceiling is higher than the general range if malice is established on the evidence.
