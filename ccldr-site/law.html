The Cannabis Act (Bill C-45)
Federal framework — in force October 17, 2018
Overview & Purpose

The Cannabis Act (S.C. 2018, c. 16) came into force on October 17, 2018, making Canada the second country in the world to federally legalize adult recreational cannabis. It creates a federal framework while expressly allowing provinces and territories to add their own restrictions and regulations — but not to relax federal minimums.

The stated purposes of the Act are threefold:

  • Protect youth — prevent access and use by minors, marketing restrictions
  • Deter criminal activity — displace illegal market through regulated legal access
  • Provide legal access — allow adults to obtain cannabis from regulated, safe sources
Key Federal Provisions
  • Adults 18+ (federally) may legally possess, use, and purchase cannabis from authorized retailers — provinces may set higher minimum ages
  • Home cultivation: Up to 4 plants per household — this is per residence, NOT per person
  • Sharing: Adults may give (not sell) up to 30 grams of dried cannabis to another adult — no payment permitted
  • Public possession: Up to 30 grams of dried cannabis (or equivalent) in a public place
  • Public consumption: Generally permitted where tobacco smoking is permitted, subject to provincial and municipal restrictions
  • Licensed producers: Cannabis for sale must originate from Health Canada-licensed producers and authorized retailers
  • Edibles, extracts, topicals: Permitted for sale beginning October 2019 under regulations (10mg THC per unit limit for edibles)
Educational Note: The Cannabis Act sets the floor — provinces can (and do) impose stricter rules. For example, Quebec raised the minimum age to 21. Always verify current provincial law in your jurisdiction. CCLDR.NET provides education, not legal advice.

Important: Exceeding public possession limits is a criminal offence under the Cannabis Act. Possessing large quantities — particularly amounts that exceed personal use thresholds — can result in a presumption of trafficking. Trafficking cannabis carries significantly more serious penalties, including imprisonment.
Penalty Overview (Simplified)
  • Simple possession (under limits): Legal for adults
  • Simple possession (over limits): Summary offence — fines or up to 5 years less a day imprisonment
  • Distribution without authorization: Hybrid offence — up to 14 years imprisonment
  • Selling to a minor: Hybrid offence — up to 14 years (aggravating factor)
  • Importing/exporting: Indictable — up to 14 years
03
Home Cultivation Rules
Growing your own — federal rules and provincial overrides
Federal Rule

The Cannabis Act permits adults to cultivate up to 4 cannabis plants per household from legally obtained seed or seedling (from an authorized retailer or licensed producer).


Per Household — NOT Per Person: A residence with 4 adults still has a maximum of 4 total plants, not 4 per person.

Provincial Bans

Quebec and Manitoba have enacted provincial legislation banning home cultivation entirely. These bans have been upheld by courts.


If you reside in Quebec or Manitoba, home cultivation is illegal regardless of the federal rule.

Additional Restrictions to Know
  • Plants must be grown from legally obtained seed or seedling (from authorized retailers) — not from the black market
  • Height restrictions — some municipalities have bylaws limiting plant height or visibility from public spaces
  • Landlord and strata rules — rental agreements and strata (condo) bylaws may prohibit growing. Provincial residential tenancy law interacts here. A landlord may be able to prohibit growing in a lease
  • Selling seeds or plants is illegal unless you hold a Health Canada licence — gifts of seed to adults within limits may be permissible, but commercial activity is not
  • Youth access — plants must be secured and not accessible to minors; failure to do so is an offence
  • Safety concerns — electrical and fire hazards from indoor grows may affect insurance coverage and give landlords grounds for action
BC, Alberta, Ontario, Saskatchewan, Nova Scotia, New Brunswick, PEI, NL, NWT, Yukon, Nunavut: 4 plants per household permitted under federal rules (absent stricter municipal bylaws).

05
Medical vs. Recreational Cannabis
Two distinct frameworks with significantly different rules
Recreational Framework
  • Governed by the Cannabis Act and provincial retail regulations
  • Maximum public possession: 30 g dried (or equivalent)
  • Maximum home grow: 4 plants per household (where permitted)
  • Purchase from authorized provincial retailers only
  • No provincial or federal health coverage for costs
  • Consumption subject to provincial and municipal rules
  • Edibles capped at 10mg THC per unit under regulations
Medical Framework (ACMPR)
  • Governed by the ACMPR (Access to Cannabis for Medical Purposes Regulations) under the Cannabis Act
  • Medical document (authorization) from a licensed healthcare practitioner required
  • Possession limit: up to 150 g (30× daily authorized amount, or as specified)
  • Personal production licence from Health Canada for growing more plants
  • Can receive cannabis directly from Licensed Producers by mail
  • Some insurance coverage available (private plans, Veterans Affairs Canada)
  • Not exempt from impairment laws while driving

Driving Impairment (Bill C-46)
The most serious legal risk for cannabis users — know this thoroughly
Critical Section: Impaired driving by cannabis is treated as seriously as alcohol impairment in Canada. Bill C-46 amended the Criminal Code to create specific THC blood concentration offences. The consequences can include a criminal record, licence suspension, fines, and imprisonment. Do not drive after consuming cannabis.
Criminal Code Blood THC Limits (Per Se Offences)
Blood THC LevelOffence TypeMaximum Penalty
2–5 ng/mL blood THC Summary offence (new offence created by C-46) Maximum fine: $1,000 (first offence)
5+ ng/mL blood THC Hybrid offence Fine on summary conviction or up to 10 years imprisonment on indictment
Any THC + 50+ mg/100mL blood alcohol Hybrid offence (combined) Same as 5+ ng/mL THC standalone
Police Powers — Roadside Detection
  • Oral Fluid Screening Device (OFSD): Police may administer a roadside saliva swab if they have reasonable suspicion of impaired driving. Detects recent cannabis use — can show positive for THC days after consumption even without impairment
  • Drug Recognition Expert (DRE) evaluation: If the officer suspects drug impairment, they may demand a DRE evaluation at the police station — a standardized 12-step evaluation protocol
  • Blood sample demand: Following DRE evaluation, police may demand a blood sample for laboratory analysis of exact ng/mL THC levels
  • Refusal: Refusing to comply with a lawful demand for OFSD, DRE, or blood sample is itself a criminal offence
Critical Legal Nuance — Education Only
  • ng/mL ≠ impairment: Blood THC concentration does not directly correspond to functional impairment. Tolerance varies enormously between individuals. Heavy, regular users may have detectable blood THC levels well after any impairment has passed
  • Medical cannabis patients: Have NO exemption from impaired driving laws. A medical authorization does not permit driving while impaired
  • Oral swab timing: Saliva swab tests can detect THC use from hours to days prior. A positive roadside swab does not legally prove impairment — blood testing is the per se standard
  • Legal challenge history: The 2–5 ng/mL offence level has been criticized scientifically as not reliably correlating with impairment for regular cannabis users. Charter challenges to these provisions are ongoing
"Do not drive after consuming cannabis."
This is the single clearest, safest, and most legally protective approach.
07
Workplace Cannabis Policies
Your rights and obligations as an employee
Safety-Sensitive Environments

Industries including oil & gas, mining, aviation, transportation, construction, and nuclear commonly maintain zero-tolerance or strict fitness-for-duty policies. These are generally upheld by courts given the safety implications.


Workers in these sectors face heightened obligations and employers have broader rights to test and restrict use.

Human Rights Obligations

The Canadian Human Rights Act and provincial equivalents impose a duty to accommodate employees with a medical cannabis need up to the point of undue hardship.


Employees cannot be terminated solely because they use medical cannabis — employers must attempt accommodation before dismissal.

Key Legal Principles
  • Stewart v Elk Valley Coal (SCC 2017): The Supreme Court upheld an employer's right to require employees to disclose addictions and substance dependencies before a safety incident occurs. Disclosure obligations in safety-sensitive roles are enforceable
  • Duty to accommodate: Employers must explore accommodations for medical cannabis users — modified duties, schedule changes, monitoring — before taking adverse employment action
  • Random drug testing: Generally NOT permitted in Canada outside demonstrably safety-sensitive work environments with a demonstrated workplace drug problem. This differs significantly from US law
  • Post-incident testing: May be permitted in safety-sensitive environments following a workplace incident or near-miss, where there is reasonable cause to suspect impairment
  • Pre-employment testing: Legal in some jurisdictions and sectors; employer must still consider accommodation obligations if a positive result is returned
  • Employee obligation — not to be impaired at work: Regardless of medical authorization, an employee has an obligation not to be impaired while performing their duties
Educational Note: Workplace cannabis law sits at the intersection of labour law, human rights law, and occupational health and safety legislation. It is highly fact-specific. If you face workplace discipline related to cannabis use, retain qualified employment counsel promptly.
08
Public Consumption Rules
Where you can — and cannot — legally consume cannabis
General Rule

Under most provincial frameworks, cannabis consumption is generally permitted in the same places where tobacco smoking is permitted — subject to provincial and municipal modification. This is the baseline, not a guarantee.

Common Prohibition Zones
  • Schools, playgrounds, daycares: Prohibited within 20 metres (distances vary by province) — near-universal restriction across all jurisdictions
  • In vehicles: Consuming or having open cannabis in a vehicle is prohibited — even if parked with the engine off. Being in a vehicle with open cannabis can expose you to impaired driving investigation
  • Enclosed public spaces: Generally prohibited in all enclosed public buildings, workplaces, shopping centres
  • Federal lands: National parks, federal buildings, airports — federal and CBSA rules apply
  • Patios and restaurant premises: Varies significantly by province and municipality
  • Hospital grounds, healthcare facilities: Prohibited
  • Near children's facilities: Recreation centres, arenas, sports fields — typically restricted
Consumption Lounges
Ontario and BC have both moved toward permitting licensed cannabis consumption lounges. These are indoor spaces licensed specifically for on-site cannabis consumption — similar to a bar but for cannabis. Still limited in number as of 2026, with regulatory frameworks continuing to evolve.
Quebec — Strictest Restrictions
Quebec only permits cannabis consumption in designated outdoor areas. Consumption is prohibited in most public spaces, near any place frequented by minors, near any healthcare or social services building, and anywhere tobacco is prohibited. The rules are among the most restrictive in Canada.

Travel Restrictions
Domestic and international travel — critical rules every Canadian should know
Domestic Air Travel (Within Canada)
  • Federally regulated airports and flights fall under federal Cannabis Act rules
  • Adults may carry up to 30 grams in carry-on or checked baggage for domestic flights
  • CATSA (screening) is focused on security threats, not cannabis detection — but if cannabis is found, it is referred to local law enforcement
  • Passengers arriving in a province with stricter rules (e.g., Quebec) remain subject to provincial law upon arrival
  • Consumption onboard aircraft is prohibited
ABSOLUTE PROHIBITION — US Border & International Travel:
  • Crossing the Canadian border with cannabis — in any direction — is a federal criminal offence under both Canadian and US law
  • Cannabis remains a Schedule I controlled substance under US federal law regardless of state-level legalization
  • Admitting to cannabis use at the US border — even historical use, even for medical purposes — can result in a lifetime inadmissibility determination (permanent ban from entering the United States)
  • US border officers are federal officers operating under federal law — Canadian provincial or federal legalization provides zero protection
  • International travel: Cannabis is illegal in the vast majority of countries. Penalties in some jurisdictions include lengthy imprisonment. Never travel internationally with cannabis.
  • Cruise ships departing Canadian ports are subject to the law of whatever country they enter — including US federal law
US Border Advice: You are under no legal obligation to answer questions about past drug use at the US border, but refusing to answer can result in denial of entry. This is a complex area — consult an immigration lawyer before attempting border crossings if you have any cannabis-related history.

Recent Legal Developments (2025–2026)
The legal landscape continues to evolve
Criminal Records — Pardons and Expungement
  • Criminal Records Act amendments: Simplified expedited pardon (record suspension) process for simple possession offences that would now be legal under the Cannabis Act
  • The Expungement of Historically Unjust Convictions Act applies to certain simple possession convictions — application process through the Parole Board of Canada
  • Pardons do not erase a record entirely but seal it from most background checks — expungement is a higher standard that destroys the record
  • As of 2026, advocacy continues for automatic expungement rather than requiring individual applications
THC Limits in Edibles
The 10mg THC per unit / 10mg per package limit for edibles remains a contentious regulatory issue. Industry and consumer advocates continue to push for increases, citing that the limit is lower than most comparable US state frameworks and drives consumers to unregulated products. Regulatory review is ongoing.
Reclassification & Taxation Debates
Federal cannabis excise tax frameworks continue to face criticism for supporting black market competition by making legal cannabis comparatively expensive. Discussions around tax reduction and regulatory streamlining are ongoing in several provinces. Watch for regulatory updates from Health Canada and provincial regulators.
Charter Challenges — Ongoing (Educational Overview)
  • Challenges to the 2–5 ng/mL THC per se driving offence continue on grounds that the limit does not reliably correlate with impairment for regular and medical cannabis users
  • ACMPR access and personal production licence challenges persist — access issues for remote and lower-income patients remain unresolved
  • Equity concerns: Evidence continues to show disproportionate enforcement of cannabis laws against Indigenous and racialized communities even post-legalization
  • Potency cap and packaging restriction challenges by industry ongoing in Federal Court
