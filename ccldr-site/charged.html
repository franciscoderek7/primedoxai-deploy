Charged with Cannabis?
Defend Yourself.

Before you say a word to police. Before you walk into court. Before you accept anything. Know exactly what your Canadian Charter rights are — and what constitutional defenses may apply to your situation.

First Steps — Right Now

If you've just been charged or arrested, the next hours matter enormously. Here's what every Canadian should do.

1

Say Nothing — Exercise Your Right to Silence

Under s.7 of the Canadian Charter, you have the right to remain silent. You are not required to answer police questions (beyond basic identification in some circumstances). Say clearly: "I am exercising my right to remain silent." Then stop talking.

2

Ask for a Lawyer — Immediately

Under s.10(b) of the Charter, you have the right to retain and instruct counsel without delay. Say: "I wish to speak to a lawyer." Police must stop questioning you and give you a reasonable opportunity to contact counsel. Do not waive this right.

3

Do Not Consent to Any Search

Under s.8 of the Charter, you are protected from unreasonable search and seizure. If asked to consent to a search, say clearly: "I do not consent to this search." Do not physically resist — just state non-consent. This preserves your Charter argument even if the search proceeds.

4

Document Everything You Can Remember

As soon as safely possible, write down: officer badge numbers, names, patrol car number, exact time and location, what was said, what was searched, what was seized, and any witnesses present. This documentation is critical for any future disclosure review or Charter application.

5

Request Full Disclosure Immediately

You have an absolute right to full Crown disclosure — police reports, notes, video, toxicology, lab certificates, informant tips used for warrants, everything. Do not plead guilty without reviewing disclosure. Many cannabis charges cannot survive full Charter scrutiny of the investigative process.

6

Educate Yourself Before Your First Court Date

The CCLDR education curriculum walks you through Charter rights, constitutional defense frameworks, how to read disclosure, how pre-trial motions work, and the landmark case law that shapes every cannabis prosecution in Canada. Knowledge changes outcomes.

Your Charter Rights

These are the constitutional protections every Canadian has when facing a cannabis charge. Understanding them is your first defense.

Charter s.7
Life, Liberty & Security of the Person
The state cannot deprive you of liberty except in accordance with the principles of fundamental justice. Denial of access to medical cannabis has been found to violate s.7 (Parker 2000, Smith 2015).
Charter s.8
Unreasonable Search & Seizure
You have a reasonable expectation of privacy. Searches without valid warrants, or warrants obtained on insufficient grounds, can be challenged. Evidence obtained in breach of s.8 may be excluded under s.24(2).
Charter s.9
Arbitrary Detention
You cannot be detained without reasonable grounds. Random stops, pretextual detentions, and prolonged investigative detentions without reasonable and probable grounds violate s.9.
Charter s.10(b)
Right to Retain Counsel
Upon detention or arrest, you have the right to be informed of your right to retain and instruct counsel without delay. Police must facilitate access to counsel before questioning you.
Charter s.11(d)
Presumption of Innocence
You are presumed innocent until proven guilty beyond a reasonable doubt. The Crown bears the burden of proving every element of the offence. You are not required to prove anything.
Charter s.24
Charter Remedies
Where your Charter rights have been violated, a court may exclude evidence (s.24(2)), stay the proceedings, or grant other appropriate remedies. Charter breaches are not academic — they have real consequences for your case.

Constitutional Defense Education

These are the educational frameworks CCLDR teaches — based on landmark Canadian case law. For any specific defense strategy, retain qualified counsel.

Medical Necessity / BENO-X Framework

If you possessed cannabis for legitimate medical purposes and the government's exemption framework was constitutionally defective at the time, the BENO-X principle (Bad Exemption = No Offense) may apply. A flawed access regime cannot support a valid prosecution.

Parker (2000 OCA) · Hitzig (2003) · Smith (2015 SCC 34) · Allard (2016 FC 236)

Section 8 — Search Charter Application

If police searched you, your vehicle, or your home without proper grounds or a valid warrant, a s.8 Charter application can seek exclusion of all evidence obtained from that search under s.24(2). Without the evidence, the prosecution often cannot proceed.

R v Grant (2009 SCC) · R v Collins (1987 SCC) · R v Tessling (2004 SCC)

Arbitrary Detention — Section 9

If the traffic stop, investigative detention, or arrest that led to the cannabis discovery was arbitrary — lacking reasonable and probable grounds — the entire investigation may be tainted. Evidence flowing from an arbitrary detention is subject to exclusion.

R v Mann (2004 SCC) · R v Clayton (2007 SCC)

Right to Counsel Breach — Section 10(b)

If police failed to properly inform you of your right to counsel, or proceeded to question you before you had a reasonable opportunity to speak with a lawyer, your s.10(b) right was violated. Statements obtained in breach of s.10(b) are routinely excluded.

R v Sinclair (2010 SCC) · R v Bartle (1994 SCC)

Disclosure Deficiency

The Crown must disclose all relevant material — including police notes, analyst certificates, informant tips used for warrants, and surveillance records. Failure to produce complete disclosure can result in stays of proceedings or exclusion of evidence.

R v Stinchcombe (1991 SCC) · R v McNeil (2009 SCC)

Entrapment

If police provided you with an opportunity to commit the offence without reasonable suspicion that you were already engaged in criminal activity, or went beyond providing an opportunity to actually inducing the offence, an entrapment argument may result in a stay of proceedings.

R v Mack (1988 SCC) · R v Ahmad (2020 SCC)

All defense frameworks presented here are for educational purposes only. Whether any defense applies to your specific facts is a question for qualified legal counsel. CCLDR does not provide educational information or representation.
