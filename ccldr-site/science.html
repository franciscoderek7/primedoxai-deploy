Educational Module — Cannabis Biology

The Science Behind Cannabis

Understanding what's actually happening in your body — cannabinoids, receptors, terpenes, and delivery methods explained.

Section 01

The Endocannabinoid System (ECS)

Your body has its own built-in cannabinoid signaling network — discovered in the early 1990s during research into how THC produces its effects.

Key insight: Phytocannabinoids (plant-derived cannabinoids like THC and CBD) mimic or modulate the body's own endocannabinoids — anandamide (AEA) and 2-arachidonoylglycerol (2-AG) — by interacting with cannabinoid receptors throughout the body.
🧠

CB1 Receptor

Primarily located in the brain and central nervous system — prefrontal cortex, hippocampus, basal ganglia, cerebellum. Responsible for psychoactive effects of THC.

⇆
🛡️

CB2 Receptor

Primarily in the immune system and peripheral tissues — spleen, tonsils, gut. Modulates inflammation and immune response. Not psychoactive.

Section 02

THC vs CBD & Other Cannabinoids

Cannabis contains over 100 distinct cannabinoids. These are the ones with the most established scientific evidence.

THC — Δ9-Tetrahydrocannabinol

Psychoactive — Partial CB1 Agonist

  • Partial agonist at CB1 receptors (produces psychoactivity)
  • Analgesic (pain relief via CB1 and opioid receptor cross-talk)
  • Antiemetic (reduces nausea and vomiting)
  • Appetite stimulant (the "munchies" — ghrelin pathway)
  • Dose-dependent anxiolytic or anxiogenic effects
  • Bronchodilator at low doses
CBD — Cannabidiol

Non-Intoxicating — CB1 Modulator

  • Negative allosteric modulator of CB1 (reduces THC's psychoactive effect)
  • Anxiolytic (reduces anxiety at appropriate doses)
  • Anti-inflammatory via CB2 and TRPV1 pathways
  • Anticonvulsant (FDA/Health Canada approved for Dravet, LGS)
  • No intoxication — does not produce a "high"
  • Inhibits CYP enzymes — drug interactions possible
Interaction: CBD can reduce THC-induced anxiety and psychoactivity. Many patients prefer a balanced THC:CBD ratio (1:1) for therapeutic use without strong intoxication.

Minor Cannabinoids

Cannabinoid Primary Properties Notable Use
CBN (Cannabinol) Sedative, mild CB1 agonist (oxidized THC) Sleep aid
CBG (Cannabigerol) Antibacterial, appetite stimulant, anti-glaucoma Emerging therapeutic
THCV (Tetrahydrocannabivarin) Appetite suppressant, may counteract THC effects Metabolic / appetite
CBC (Cannabichromene) Anti-inflammatory, antidepressant, neurogenesis Inflammation
Entourage Effect: Whole-plant cannabis containing the full spectrum of cannabinoids and terpenes may produce synergistic effects greater than isolated compounds — this is the scientific basis for preferring full-spectrum or broad-spectrum products over isolates for many conditions.
Section 03

Indica vs Sativa: The Myth

One of the most pervasive misconceptions in cannabis culture — and the science has caught up.

⚠ Marketing Reality

Indica and Sativa are botanical classifications describing a plant's physical characteristics — leaf shape, plant height, geographic origin, and growth patterns. They are not reliable predictors of the effects you will experience. A dispensary labeling something "indica for relaxation" and "sativa for energy" is making a claim that is not supported by the science.

What Actually Predicts Effects

  • Cannabinoid profile (THC:CBD ratio)
  • Terpene content and concentrations
  • Individual biology, metabolism & tolerance
  • Set and setting (mindset and environment)
  • Consumption method and dose

"Couch-Lock" Explained

The sedating "body high" commonly attributed to indicas is actually correlated with high myrcene content — a terpene. You can find high-myrcene sativas and low-myrcene indicas. Terpenes matter far more than the indica/sativa label.

Chemotypes — The Accurate Classification

Type I
High THC / Low CBD

Most recreational cannabis. Strong psychoactivity. Used for pain, nausea, sleep. Higher risk profile.

Type II
Balanced THC:CBD

Mixed ratio — e.g., 1:1 or 1:2. Reduced psychoactivity from CBD modulation. Often preferred for medical use.

Type III
High CBD / Low THC

Hemp-dominant. Non-intoxicating. Used for inflammation, anxiety, seizure disorders. CBD isolates come from here.

Section 04

Terpenes & The Entourage Effect

Terpenes are aromatic compounds found throughout nature — in cannabis they do far more than create scent. They modulate cannabinoid effects and have their own biological activity.

Terpene Aroma Profile Effects & Properties Also Found In Notes
Myrcene Earthy, mango, musky Sedating, muscle relaxant, analgesic Hops, mango, lemongrass The "couch-lock" terpene — most abundant in cannabis
Limonene Citrus, lemon, orange Uplifting, anti-anxiety, antidepressant, antifungal Citrus peel, juniper Associated with mood elevation
α-Pinene Pine, fresh forest Alerting, memory retention, anti-inflammatory, bronchodilator Pine trees, rosemary May counteract THC short-term memory effects
Linalool Floral, lavender, spice Anxiolytic, sedating, anti-inflammatory, analgesic Lavender, coriander Same compound responsible for lavender's calming effects
β-Caryophyllene Spicy, pepper, woody Anti-inflammatory, analgesic, CB2 agonist Black pepper, cloves, hops Only known terpene that directly activates cannabinoid receptors (CB2)
Humulene Earthy, hoppy, woody Appetite suppressant, anti-inflammatory, antibacterial Hops, sage, ginseng Contrasts with THC's appetite-stimulating effect
Terpinolene Floral, herbal, fresh Antioxidant, mildly sedating, antibacterial Sage, rosemary, apples Less common but found in some sativa-labeled strains
Entourage Effect in Practice: Research suggests that the combination of terpenes and cannabinoids produces effects that neither can achieve alone. This is why standardized whole-plant extracts may outperform isolated THC or CBD for some conditions — and why reading the terpene profile of your product matters more than the strain name.
Section 05

Delivery Methods Comparison

How you consume cannabis fundamentally changes the onset, duration, and intensity of effects — and the health risk profile.

Method Onset Duration Bioavailability Advantages Disadvantages
🔥 Inhalation (Smoking) 1–3 min 2–3 hr 10–35% Rapid onset; easy dose titration; immediate relief Respiratory risk from combustion; odour; social stigma
💨 Vaporization 1–5 min 2–3 hr 10–35% Fewer combustion byproducts than smoking; precise temperature control Requires device and maintenance; upfront cost
🍪 Oral (Edibles) 30–120 min 4–8 hr 4–20% Discreet; long-lasting relief; no inhalation Highly unpredictable onset; easy to over-consume; THC converts to 11-OH-THC (more potent)
💧 Sublingual (Tinctures) 15–45 min 4–6 hr 13–19% Faster than oral; dose control; discreet Taste may be unpleasant; variable absorption based on formulation
🧴 Topical 15–45 min 2–4 hr Low (local) Non-intoxicating (in most cases); targeted local relief Effect limited to application site; transdermal patches can cause systemic effects
💊 Suppository 10–15 min 4–8 hr ~13.5% Useful when oral route unavailable; specialized palliative use Limited evidence; specialized medical application; not widely used
Edible Warning: The most common cannabis-related emergency room visits involve edible overconsumption. The delayed onset (up to 2 hours) causes people to re-dose, then experience the combined effect. Wait at least 2 hours before considering an additional dose.
Section 06

Dosage Guidelines

There is no universal dose. Individual variation is significant — metabolism, tolerance, body weight, route of administration, and prior cannabis use all affect response.

The Golden Rule: Start Low, Go Slow. Begin with the lowest effective dose, wait the full onset time, and increase gradually over days or weeks — not within a single session.

THC Dosing (oral unless noted)

Microdose / Beginner
1–2.5 mg
Minimal intoxication. For first-time users or those sensitive to THC. Often provides relief without noticeable psychoactivity.
Low / Moderate Experience
2.5–15 mg
Standard recreational and therapeutic range. Noticeable effects. Health Canada's 10mg single serving standard for regulated products.
Experienced User
15–30 mg
Strong effects. Significant tolerance usually required. Not recommended for new users.
Medical High-Dose
30 mg+
Medical purposes only, under healthcare provider supervision. Associated with higher risk of adverse effects without tolerance.

CBD Dosing

CBD does not produce intoxication — there is no risk of "taking too much" in the same sense as THC. However, higher doses are required for clinical effects and can interact with medications.

General Wellness
10–30 mg
Daily supplemental use. Anti-anxiety, mild anti-inflammatory.
Therapeutic
30–100 mg
Pain, inflammation, sleep. Range studied in clinical trials.
Clinical (Seizures)
100–600 mg
Doses used in Epidiolex trials for epilepsy. Requires medical supervision — drug interactions at these levels.

⚠ Individual Variation Factors

The following factors significantly affect cannabis response: liver metabolism (CYP2C9/CYP3A4 enzyme activity), body composition (cannabinoids are fat-soluble), tolerance from prior use, endocannabinoid system baseline, concurrent medications, and method of consumption. What works for one person may be completely different for another — this is not a placebo effect, it is pharmacogenomics.
