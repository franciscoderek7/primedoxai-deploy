Opening Declaration

Preamble

✦   ✦   ✦

We, the people who have suffered under unjust cannabis prohibition, who have been imprisoned for medicine, denied employment for our beliefs, and stripped of property without cause — establish this Constitution not to rebel against law, but to define it. Built on 20 years of precedent from Parker to Allard to Smith, this document declares the rights no legislature may lawfully abrogate. We write not in anger, but in the unyielding conviction that human dignity is not a privilege granted by statute, but a right that precedes all government. This Constitution is alive. It breathes with each case decided, each right defended, each person freed. It belongs to all who suffer, all who fight, and all who refuse to be silent.

The Seven Foundational Rights

The Seven Articles

Article I Right to Cultivate +

Every person has the right to cultivate cannabis for personal use. No government may restrict cultivation below a threshold that ensures practical access. Regulatory schemes that make personal cultivation impossible in practice — through space limits, licensing barriers, or cost — are constitutionally void. Access must be real, not theoretical.

Grounded in: Allard v. Canada, 2016 FC 236  |  R. v. Smith, 2015 SCC 34  |  Charter s.7

Article II Right to Medicine +

Cannabis as medicine is a constitutional right under s.7 of the Charter. Denial of safe, legal access constitutes a violation of the right to life, liberty, and security of the person. No administrative inconvenience, budgetary concern, or stigma may override this right. Where medical need is established, access is not discretionary — it is mandatory.

Grounded in: R. v. Parker, 2000 ONCA 3939  |  R. v. Mernagh, 2013  |  Charter s.7

Article III Right to Privacy +

Cultivation, possession, and consumption within a private dwelling is protected from unreasonable search and seizure under the equivalent of Charter s.8. No warrant may issue without demonstrated probable cause, specifically stated, judicially authorized. Evidence obtained in violation of this Article is excluded absolutely — there is no balancing test where a home is violated without lawful authority.

Grounded in: R. v. Grant, 2009 SCC 32  |  R. v. Stairs, 2022 SCC 11  |  Charter s.8

Article IV Right to Commerce +

Commerce in cannabis products, where regulated and licensed, must be conducted with full due process, transparent regulatory criteria, and without arbitrary denial of licenses or permits. Regulatory bodies must publish objective criteria, provide written reasons for all denials, and offer a meaningful right of appeal. Regulatory capture — the use of licensing power to protect incumbents — is a constitutional violation.

Grounded in: Administrative law principles  |  Charter s.7 (economic liberty)  |  Cannabis Act, S.C. 2018, c. 16

Article V Right to Due Process +

Every person charged with a cannabis offence is entitled to full disclosure, the right to counsel (s.10(b)), and a fair hearing before an impartial tribunal. Procedural shortcuts — delayed disclosure, inadequate legal aid, administrative detention — are unconstitutional. Justice delayed is justice denied. This Constitution demands swift, fair, and fully resourced proceedings in every cannabis matter without exception.

Grounded in: R. v. Jordan, 2016 SCC 27  |  R. v. Stinchcombe, 1991 SCC  |  Charter s.10(b), s.11

Article VI Right to Equal Treatment +

The equal treatment guarantee (Charter s.15 equivalent) prohibits enforcement policies that disproportionately target marginalized communities. Statistical disparate impact is evidence of systemic violation. Where data demonstrates that enforcement falls unequally — by race, postal code, income, or age — the burden shifts to the state to justify that inequality. Proof of disparity is proof of unconstitutionality unless rebutted by compelling state evidence.

Grounded in: Charter s.15  |  Mouvement laïque québécois v. Saguenay, 2015  |  R. v. Le, 2019 SCC 34

Article VII Right to Constitutional Remedy +

Every violation of this Constitution carries a mandatory remedy — exclusion of evidence, stay of proceedings, damages, or declaration — at the discretion of the presiding justice. No violation is too minor for remedy. Courts that decline to remedy proven constitutional violations do violence to the rule of law itself. Where government cannot offer justification under s.1, remedy is automatic and complete. Rights without remedies are not rights at all.

Grounded in: Charter s.24  |  R. v. Ward, 2010 SCC 27  |  Henry v. British Columbia (AG), 2015 SCC 24
