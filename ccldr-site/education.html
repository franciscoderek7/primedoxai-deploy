Track 1 — Medical

Medical Marijuana Law

Canada's medical cannabis framework was built case-by-case through constitutional battles. Understanding this history is essential to understanding what the courts actually decided — and what rights exist today.

Regulatory History of Medical Access in Canada

Pre-2001 Prohibition — Prior to 2001, cannabis was entirely prohibited under the Narcotic Control Act and later the Controlled Drugs and Substances Act (CDSA). There were no provisions for medical use. Patients relying on cannabis for serious medical conditions faced criminal prosecution.

MMAR 2001 (Marihuana Medical Access Regulations) — Following the R v Parker ruling, the federal government introduced the MMAR in July 2001 — the first regulated framework permitting possession and production of cannabis for individuals with serious medical conditions. Access required physician approval and a Health Canada licence.

MMPR 2013 (Marihuana for Medical Purposes Regulations) — The MMPR shifted production to licensed commercial producers, eliminating personal production licences. Courts would later find this shift problematic, as it removed rights previously granted under the MMAR.

ACMPR 2016 (Access to Cannabis for Medical Purposes Regulations) — Following the Allard v Canada ruling, the ACMPR restored personal and designated production. Patients could once again grow their own medicine at home. The ACMPR remained in force until legalization.

Landmark Cases — Medical Marijuana
2000
R v Parker, 2000 CanLII 5762 (ON CA)
The Ontario Court of Appeal found that the prohibition on cannabis possession violated Section 7 of the Charter for individuals with medical necessity. The court struck down the prohibition and ordered Parliament to create a medical exemption. This ruling directly led to the MMAR 2001 and is the foundation of all subsequent medical cannabis law in Canada.
2003
R v Hitzig, 2003 CanLII 44611 (ON CA)
The Ontario Court of Appeal struck down portions of the MMAR as unconstitutional, finding that the designated-producer supply system was unworkable and failed to provide a genuinely accessible legal supply. The ruling forced the government to amend the MMAR to broaden access.
2015
R v Smith, 2015 SCC 34
The Supreme Court of Canada unanimously held that the prohibition on cannabis derivatives (oils, edibles) for medical purposes violated Section 7 of the Charter. The right to possess medical cannabis could not be restricted to dried plant material. This ruling expanded medical access to all forms of cannabis — a significant constitutional victory.
Current Rights — ACMPR Licence Process

Under current federal law, individuals with medical authorization may possess cannabis beyond standard limits and may produce a personal supply. Key rights include:

  • ✓Possession for personal medical use above the public limit (30g) with a medical document
  • ✓Personal production licence — calculated at 5 plants per gram of daily authorized dose (indoor)
  • ✓Designated person production — a named individual may produce on your behalf
  • ✓Access to all cannabis products and forms for medical purposes
Section 7 of the Charter: "Everyone has the right to life, liberty and security of the person and the right not to be deprived thereof except in accordance with the principles of fundamental justice." Courts have held that denying access to medically necessary cannabis engages and violates this right.
Track 2 — Current Law

Current Cannabis Law

Legalization under Bill C-45 in 2018 did not eliminate constitutional issues. It created a new regulated framework that raised its own Charter questions — and left many prior injustices unresolved.

Cannabis Act — Bill C-45 (October 17, 2018)

The Cannabis Act, S.C. 2018, c. 16, came into force on October 17, 2018, establishing the first legal framework for recreational cannabis in Canada. Key elements:

  • ✓Federal framework — governs production, distribution, sale, possession, and import/export
  • ✓Provincial authority — each province controls retail, age limits (minimum 18/19 federal), consumption rules
  • ✓Strict restrictions on marketing and promotion — industry argues these violate Section 2(b) freedom of expression
  • ✓Criminal penalties remain for exceeding limits, unlicensed sale, and distribution to minors

Key Charter Arguments Active Post-Legalization
S.8
Section 8 — Unreasonable Search and Seizure
Police demand for saliva or blood samples without reasonable grounds. Drug recognition evaluator (DRE) protocols under challenge. Roadside testing devices — accuracy and admissibility contested.
S.7
Section 7 — Liberty and Overbreadth
Criminal penalties for exceeding possession limits by small amounts. Application of pre-legalization criminal records against post-legalization conduct — record suspension barriers remain contested.
C-46
Bill C-46 — Impaired Driving Provisions
Bill C-46 amended the Criminal Code to create per se limits for THC (2ng/mL blood serum). Multiple Charter challenges are active: the per se limit does not correlate with impairment, violating Section 7 (overbreadth) and potentially Section 15 (discriminatory impact on medical users). Scientific evidence of arbitrary limit is a live constitutional issue.
S.15
Section 15 — Equality Rights
Disproportionate enforcement of cannabis laws against Indigenous and Black Canadians continues post-legalization. Equality challenges based on documented systemic discrimination in enforcement remain an active area of litigation and advocacy.
Track 3 — Constitutional History

History 2000–Present

A quarter-century of constitutional battles, regulatory shifts, landmark victories, and ongoing challenges. This is the timeline that built the rights that exist today.

2000
R v Parker — Section 7 Victory
The Ontario Court of Appeal rules in favour of Terrance Parker, an epileptic patient. The court strikes down the blanket prohibition on cannabis as a violation of Section 7 of the Charter, requiring Parliament to create a medical exemption. The foundation of all Canadian medical cannabis law is laid.
2001
MMAR — First Regulated Medical Access
The Marihuana Medical Access Regulations are enacted in direct response to Parker. For the first time, seriously ill Canadians can apply for a licence to possess and produce cannabis for medical purposes. The framework is limited and bureaucratically cumbersome, but it is a constitutional milestone.
2003
R v Hitzig — MMAR Supply Rules Struck Down
The Ontario Court of Appeal finds the designated-producer supply system in the MMAR constitutionally inadequate. The practical inaccessibility of legal supply means the exemption does not cure the Charter violation. The government is forced to amend the MMAR to expand access to independent supply networks.
2006
CCLDR Founded by Derek Francisco
Derek Francisco (Doc Weedlaw) establishes the Cannabis Constitutional Legal Defense Resource (CCLDR). The platform begins building what will become the largest cannabis constitutional law database in Canada, providing educational resources to patients, individuals facing charges, and self-represented litigants.
2012
Allard v Canada Begins
Allard v Canada is filed after the MMPR (2013 regulations) eliminates personal production licences, forcing all patients to purchase from licensed commercial producers. The plaintiffs argue this removes constitutionally protected rights established under the MMAR and violates Section 7 of the Charter.
2015
R v Smith — Cannabis Derivatives Freed
The Supreme Court of Canada unanimously holds in R v Smith, 2015 SCC 34, that restricting medical cannabis access to dried plant material violates Section 7. Oils, resins, edibles, and other derivatives are constitutionally protected for medical use. A unanimous SCC ruling for cannabis rights.
2016
ACMPR — Grow-Your-Own Rights Restored
Following the federal court ruling in Allard v Canada, which found the MMPR violated Section 7, the government introduces the Access to Cannabis for Medical Purposes Regulations (ACMPR). Personal and designated production rights are restored. Patients can once again grow their own medicine — a direct constitutional remedy.
2018
Bill C-45 — The Cannabis Act — Legalization
On October 17, 2018, Canada becomes the second country in the world to federally legalize cannabis for adult recreational use. Bill C-45, the Cannabis Act, creates a new regulatory framework. Medical access is preserved under new regulations. However, criminal penalties remain for many activities, and enforcement disparities persist.
2018
Bill C-46 — Impaired Driving Laws Challenged
Bill C-46 creates per se blood-THC limits for impaired driving. Critics immediately identify that the 2ng/mL THC limit is scientifically unsound — THC remains in blood long after impairment has dissipated, disproportionately affecting medical cannabis users. Constitutional challenges begin across multiple provinces.
2023+
Post-Legalization Charter Challenges
The fight is not over. Active challenges include: record suspension barriers for pre-legalization cannabis convictions; Section 15 equality challenges over racially disparate enforcement post-legalization; Bill C-46 impaired driving per se limits; and marketing/promotion restrictions that the cannabis industry argues violate Section 2(b) freedom of expression. The constitutional story of cannabis in Canada continues.
